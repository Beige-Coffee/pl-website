# Lightning Payments Overview

Welcome! If you’re reading this, then you’ve chosen to embark on a journey to learn how payments are routed over bitcoin’s Lightning Network. This course builds on material from the [Intro to Payment Channels](/lightning-tutorial) and [Secure Communication & Lightning's Noise Protocol](/noise-tutorial) tutorials. If you're a little rusty on either, no worries, we’ll link back to them when applicable if you need a refresher.


Before we get into any fun cryptography, we need to set the scene by reviewing what a Lightning payment actually *is* and what information needs to be transmitted across nodes to enable a successful payment. Once we paint that picture, the rest of the course will be much more intuitive.

## A payment path is a sequence of channels

As we learned in [Intro to Payment Channels](/lightning-tutorial), the Lightning Network is, at its core, a graph of **payment channels** between nodes. If **Alice** wants to pay **Dave**, but Alice and Dave don't share a channel, then all Alice needs to do is find a path through to Dave via existing channels that connect them. For our running example, **Bob** has a channel with both Alice and **Charlie**, and Charlie has a channel with both Bob and Dave. So the payment travels Alice → Bob → Charlie → Dave, hopping across one channel at a time.

You can see their path is embedded within the broader Lightning ecosystem. ## Each channel is a pair of commitment transactions

Now, let's dig into the details. After all, to really understand how a Lightning payment works, you'll need to understand how commitment transactions are updated. If you need a full deep-dive into this topic, see [Intro to Payment Channels](/lightning-tutorial). As a brief refresher, **every Lightning channel is anchored by a pair of commitment transactions**, one held by each channel partner. The input to both commitment transactions is the **Funding UTXO**, which locks the channel balance to a shared 2-of-2 multisig. They differ only in which output is delayed (the local party's) and which is immediately spendable (the counterparty's). Remember, the asymmetric delay ensures that, if one party attempts to cheat by publishing an old state, their counterparty has time to sweep all of their funds. Ouch!

Take a look at the visual below. Click play and watch a 10,000-sat payment travel through the network. At each hop, *both* commitment transactions in the active channel update at once: an <g>HTLC</g> output appears, locked to the same payment hash and a decrementing expiry locktime. Remember, the timelock must expire at the last node in the payment path *first*, as this helps ensure that the payment will not partially succeed or fail. When the <g>preimage</g> propagates back, the HTLC unwinds and the sats move into the recipient's `to_local`. Notice that **Bob and Charlie each hold *two* commitment transactions**, one for each of their payment channels.
