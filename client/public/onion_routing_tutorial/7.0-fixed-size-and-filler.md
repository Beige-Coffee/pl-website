# The Fixed-Size Packet

Nice! We've loaded up our cryptographic toolkit! At this point, we've seen that each hop will have its own shared secret with Alice, derived deterministically from a single session key, and, from each shared secret, we can derive five named keys (`rho`, `mu`, `um`, `ammag`, and `pad`), each with a specific purpose.

With this in mind, let's see how everything is put into practice to create an onion packet that can be privately routed across the network *without leaking any information*.

## Without leaking any information

So far in this course, we've been leaking some pretty important information in our ongoing examples and visuals! In our onion routing packet constructions, every node in the route receives the packet and pulls out the payment information relevant to them. Then it simply forwards the *rest* of the packet onwards.

Notice anything? At every hop, the packet size *shrinks*. For instance, Bob, the first hop, receives a 3-hop payload packet (~1,300 bytes), peels his hop payload, and forwards a 2-hop payload packet to Charlie (~870 bytes). Charlie peels his and forwards a 1-hop payload packet to Dave (~430 bytes). Something seems wrong here...

Remember the privacy properties we reviewed in chapter 3? Namely, **intermediate hops should not know where they are in the path**. This rule is breaking right in front of our eyes!

## The Sphinx fix: every packet is 1,366 bytes

To ensure that each intermediate hop cannot discern where it is in the route, the Lightning network uses the **Sphinx packet format**, which fixes the size of each onion packet at exactly **1,366 bytes** for **every hop**. In other words, in our example, the packets that Bob, Charlie, and Dave receive will all be exactly 1,366 bytes.

How do we ensure this? Well, when Bob peels his hop payload off the front, he doesn't turn around and send the rest of the packet downstream. Instead, he shifts the inner contents forward to fill the gap, and **pads the trailing space** with bytes that *look* like more encrypted onion data.

That last part is worth stressing. The padding can't just be *anything* that fills the gap to 1,366 bytes. Two requirements pin down what those bytes have to be:

- **They have to look like real ciphertext**, indistinguishable from the encrypted hop payloads that came before. If they didn't, a forwarder could still tell where the "real" content ends and the "padding" begins, and we'd be right back to leaking position from the contents.
- **They have to satisfy each downstream hop's HMAC verification.** Each forwarder, before doing anything else, recomputes an HMAC over the encrypted bytes it received and rejects the packet if the HMAC doesn't match. So the padding bytes can't just *look* random; they have to be the *specific* bytes Alice baked each hop's HMAC over.

That second requirement is what makes this hard. Bob can't just generate random-looking bytes on his own, because he doesn't know what Charlie's HMAC was computed over (Charlie's HMAC depends on Charlie's `rho` and `mu` keys, which Bob doesn't have). So where do the right bytes come from?

## Three approaches, one survivor

Let's compare three approaches side-by-side. Click through the tabs and watch what happens at Charlie's HMAC check.

Two of these fail and one works. The pattern is the same in both failures: Alice baked each forwarder's HMAC over a *specific* sequence of bytes, and neither zeros nor random padding match that sequence. The HMAC verifies a single deterministic answer, and only the right bytes produce it.

The fix that does work is for **Alice to precompute filler bytes** that, when each forwarder's `rho` keystream extends past the 1,300-byte boundary during peeling, naturally produce the bytes each downstream hop's HMAC expects. Bob doesn't generate the trailing bytes himself; he just shifts the inner content forward, and the math falls out. The bytes that show up in the trailing region are exactly what Alice baked the filler to be.

Let's see how Alice computes those bytes.

These precomputed bytes are called the **filler**. We're going to compute them now.

The key insight: Bob's "shift forward and pad the gap" operation is mathematically identical to **extending Bob's `rho` keystream past 1,300 bytes**, XOR-ing it across both the original 1,300 bytes *and* an extra <m>s_B</m> bytes of zeros. Phrased that way, the trailing bytes of Bob's outgoing packet are deterministic: they're whatever Bob's *extended* keystream produces over a region of zeros.

So if Alice precomputes the cumulative XOR of every hop's extended `rho` keystream (in the right positions), she gets bytes that, after every forwarder peels in turn, leave exactly what each downstream HMAC expects. Each forwarder's peel cancels their layer of the filler, like an onion in reverse.

## The filler algorithm

For our route Alice → Bob → Charlie → Dave, Alice has to handle Bob's shift (which affects what Charlie sees in *his* trailing positions) and Charlie's shift (which affects what Dave sees in *his* trailing positions). Dave doesn't shift anything (he's the final hop), so no filler is generated for the final hop.

After this step, the filler contains the keystream pattern that *will* appear at Charlie's trailing positions if Bob's shift is treated as a virtual extension of Bob's keystream.

**Step 3: For Charlie (the second forwarder).**

Prepend <m>s_C</m> more zero bytes to the *front* of the filler. Now the filler has length <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m>, representing trailing positions that span back two hops.

## Another way to see it

If the algorithm above made your head hurt, here's an equivalent framing. Imagine running peeling in reverse, from Dave back toward Bob. Dave's view of the packet has its trailing bytes come from somewhere innocuous (Alice will splice them in directly during the innermost wrap, in chapter 6). Charlie's shift, when it happens, dropped its leading <m>s_C</m> bytes off the front and would have introduced some bytes at the back. Those bytes are exactly Charlie's *extended rho keystream*, since the bytes Bob shifted in were XORed with Charlie's rho keystream during construction, and Charlie applied that same keystream during peeling.

Working backward like this, each hop's trailing-byte pattern is the previous hop's extended keystream stacked on top of zeros. The forward-direction algorithm above produces the exact same bytes; it just builds them up incrementally instead of running peeling backward.

If you can mentally hold the picture of "each hop's `rho` keystream, layered on top of the next hop's keystream, in the right positions," you've got the filler.

<details>
<summary>What if the filler bytes are wrong?</summary>

If a hop's trailing bytes don't match what its `rho` keystream-XOR-then-slide-forward produces, the next hop's HMAC verification will fail. The forwarder rejects the packet with `invalid_onion_hmac`. The error onion comes back to Alice, who has both the failure and the route to debug.

In a correct implementation, this never happens since filler is fully deterministic given the route and the rho keys. But it's the kind of bug that's easy to introduce when implementing Sphinx for the first time: off-by-one errors on the keystream slicing, or padding the wrong direction, will produce a packet that looks well-formed but fails at the very first hop. The test suite for this chapter's exercise checks against a reference implementation to catch these mistakes.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Why doesn't the final hop need filler?</summary>

The final hop (Dave) doesn't forward, so he doesn't need to produce a packet that the *next* hop's HMAC can verify. There's no next hop. After Dave decrypts his hop payload, he reads his TLV payload (which contains `payment_data` instead of a `short_channel_id`), claims the HTLC by revealing the preimage, and that's the end of the line.

</details>
