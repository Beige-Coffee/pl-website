# Pathfinding 101

In the last section, we saw Alice send a 10,000 sat <g>HTLC</g> through Bob → Charlie → Dave. But how did Alice know that was the ideal path?

To answer that question, it's helpful to start by reviewing how nodes on the Lightning Network learn about each other's presence and preferences for forwarding payments.

## Building a Lightning Network Graph

When Alice first joins the Lightning Network, she likely won't have a very clear view of the network. Since Lightning is decentralized, there is no central authority that she can ping to fetch a robust and complete view. Instead, she will have to join the peer-to-peer **gossip protocol**, which defines how Lightning nodes should advertise their payment channels, which features they support, and their fee structure.

The full list of gossip messages can be found in [BOLT 7](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/07-routing-gossip.md). For this course, we'll focus on the following:
- **`channel_announcement`**: Published when a channel opens. It links the channel to the on-chain 2-of-2 multisig output, and it contains signatures from both parties node keys and funding keys. Together these let other nodes verify that the two parties really own the on-chain Funding Transaction.
- **`channel_update`**: Published whenever a forwarder sets or changes its forwarding policy and fee structure. Each `channel_update` is signed, and contains several fields that help peers update their graph and evaluate the ideal path to send a payment:
  - **`short_channel_id`**: Identifier for the channel.
  - **`timestamp`**: Lets peers keep only the most recent update per channel.
  - **`chain_hash`**: Tells peers which network (mainnet, testnet, etc.) this update belongs to.
  - **`channel_flags`**: Indicates which side of the channel is publishing this update.
  - **`htlc_minimum_msat`** / **`htlc_maximum_msat`**: The smallest and largest HTLCs this forwarder is willing to relay.
  - **`fee_base_msat`** (often shortened to `base_fee`): A flat fee paid for every forwarded HTLC regardless of size.
  - **`fee_proportional_millionths`** (often shortened to `ppm` or `fee_rate`): A proportional fee, in parts per million of the forwarded amount.
  - **`cltv_expiry_delta`**: The number of blocks of safety margin the forwarder requires between the timelock on its *incoming* HTLC and the timelock on its *outgoing* HTLC. This buffer gives the forwarder enough time to claim its incoming HTLC (on-chain or off-chain) if the downstream hop stalls or only reveals the <g>preimage</g> at the very last second. This is actually quite important! If you're unfamiliar and want to dig into why this is so crucial, jump to the [optional deep dive on the CLTV race condition](#cltv-race-condition) at the bottom of this chapter!

For the rest of this chapter we'll focus on the three fields that are both important for pathfinding and understanding Lightning payments: `fee_base_msat`, `fee_proportional_millionths`, and `cltv_expiry_delta`.

## Choosing a payment path

As you can see, Alice has multiple potential paths to choose from. To decide which is "best", Alice will use a **pathfinding algorithm**.

Major Lightning implementation use a variant of **Dijkstra's algorithm**, a classic shortest-path search, over the gossip graph. Each one weights channels a little differently, but they all blend the channel's fee with an estimate of how likely the payment is to actually succeed. The links below dig into each major implementation's pathfinding approach.

- **[LND](https://docs.lightning.engineering/lightning-network-tools/lnd/pathfinding)**
- **[Core Lightning](https://docs.corelightning.org/reference/renepay)**
- **[LDK](https://lightningdevkit.org/blog/ldk-pathfinding/)**
- **[Eclair](https://github.com/ACINQ/eclair)**

For the rest of this chapter, we'll build our intuition for how fees and timelocks impact pathfinding by calculating the total fees and timelocks required for each of the three paths above.

Alice has to pay this fee **in addition to** the actual payment amount. These fees are what compensate Lightning operators for tying up their liquidity while the payment is in flight. Without fees, the Lightning Network would rely on altruism for forwarding payments, which, while generous, is not a robust incentive mechanism!

One subtlety before we move on: that proportional fee is charged on the amount a hop *forwards*, and each hop has to forward enough to cover the fees of every hop downstream of it. So Alice can't just add the fees up left to right. She has to work them out from Dave **backwards**: Dave's amount is fixed by the invoice, and each hop upstream tacks its own fee on top of everything below it. (You'll see this on Route B below, where the first forwarder actually forwards *more* than 400,000 sats to cover the second forwarder's fee.)

## Optional deep dive: the CLTV race condition

<details open>
<summary>How forwarders avoid getting burned by stalls and last-second preimage reveals</summary>

A nuanced thinker might say.... "but what if an intermediary node doesn't cooperate and, instead, closes the channel on-chain or only reveals the preimage at the very last second?" Great question! This is the reason why the HTLC at the final hop is set to expire *first*, with each upstream contract expiring later in reverse order. This way, if any intermediary hop closes their channel on-chain or stalls until the very last second to reveal the preimage, their counterparty still has time to grab the preimage and claim funds from *their* incoming HTLC.

For instance, in our ongoing example, imagine all contracts expire at the same time and Charlie waits until the last second to reveal the preimage. If Charlie uses it to claim Bob's HTLC, Bob may not have enough time to turn around and claim his HTLC from Alice before her timelock expires. In this case, Alice would claw her funds back via the timeout path, *and* Dave would still have been paid (since Charlie already paid him). Bob, caught in the middle, eats the loss! The reverse-ordered expiries exist precisely to prevent this.

Then tighten the expiries toward each other (giving a forwarder less cushion) and step through again to see a hop run out of time (depending on which cushion you tighten, the hop left short may differ from Bob in the example above).
