# Forwarding & Validation

Great, Bob can now peel a layer and produce the packet for the next hop. Is that all he needs to do? Not quite! Before forwarding the onion packet, Bob needs to enact Bitcoin's ethos of "don't trust, verify". In other words, he needs to verify the **integrity** of the packet and do a few **policy checks**, such as making sure the payment pays his fee and that the CLTV timelock delta is sufficient. Remember, these checks are vital for Bob. He is ultimately committing his own liquidity in the outgoing HTLC, so he needs to make sure he is compensated and that his funds are safe.

It's also worth noting that most of these checks are not optional. BOLT 4 defines the onion checks and the exact failure codes Bob should return, both for these and for a suite of additional failures that can pop up during a payment. BOLT 2 lists the [forwarding requirements](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/02-peer-protocol.md#forwarding-htlcs), and BOLT 7's `channel_update` is where Bob advertises the fee and timelock he'll enforce.

## The processing order: verify, then peel

Before Bob goes through the trouble of peeling the onion, he needs to make sure it's legit first. In other words, he needs to check the *integrity* of the message. Below, you'll see the steps Bob takes when receiving a packet, starting from verifying the HMAC and then ending with his decision of how to process the request.

1. **Length and version checks.** If the packet isn't 1,366 bytes or the version byte isn't `0x00`, Bob rejects it immediately. Why? Per BOLT 4, the only currently acceptable version is `0x00` and the length must be 1,366.
2. **HMAC verification (the integrity check).** Next, Bob recomputes `HMAC-SHA256(mu_i, hop_payloads || associated_data)` with his own `mu` key and compares it against the `hmac` field on the packet. If they don't match, then the bytes may have been tampered with, so Bob rejects them with `invalid_onion_hmac`.
3. **Peel.** If the HMAC verifies, then Bob decrypts the onion (this is the chapter 9 peel we just reviewed!). He derives `rho`, XORs the extended keystream across the buffer, and lifts his hop payload off the front.
4. **Parse the TLV payload.** Next, Bob reads the TLV records inside his freshly-decrypted hop payload to determine what he's being asked to do. For example, is there a `short_channel_id`? If so, then he's a forwarder and needs to pass the onion to another hop. If there is `payment_data` and no `short_channel_id`, then Bob is the destination, so the onion ends with him.
5. **Sanity-check fees and timelocks (the policy check).** If Bob is forwarding, he needs to make sure he's making some money and that it's not risky for him to forward the payment. For instance, does the incoming <g>HTLC</g> cover `amt_to_forward` plus his own fee? And does its <g>CLTV</g> sit far enough above `outgoing_cltv_value` to leave him his required delta, ensuring he has time to unwind the HTLC if the next hop waits until the last second to settle the payment? Those two are the big ones, but not the whole list. Bob also confirms he can actually make the forward, like checking that `short_channel_id` points to one of his channels and that the channel is online with enough liquidity. If any check fails, he doesn't forward, and he fails the HTLC instead.
6. **Forward or settle.** If everything checks out, Bob either passes the onion packet on and sets up an outgoing HTLC (if he's a forwarder) or settles it himself (if he's the destination). If any check failed, Bob fails the HTLC with the appropriate error code, which, as we'll see in chapter 11, makes its way back to Alice.

## Reading the instructions: the TLV payload

Once Bob has verified and peeled, he needs to know what he's actually being asked to do. For instance, should he pass the payment along, or is he the final recipient? To answer this, he'll need to process the `payload_bytes` and extract the bigsize-prefixed TLV records. The full set of per-hop TLV types are listed in BOLT 4's [Payload Format](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/04-onion-routing.md#packet-structure). For this section, we'll review the main records that are present for a standard, unblinded payment:

- Type 2: `amt_to_forward` (msat).
- Type 4: `outgoing_cltv_value` (block height).
- Type 6: `short_channel_id` (intermediate hops only).
- Type 8: `payment_data` (final hop only): `payment_secret` + `total_msat`.

Bob will read the TLV records in order (their type numbers have to increase as he goes). If `short_channel_id` is there, then Bob is a forwarding node. If `payment_data` is there but `short_channel_id` is not, then Bob is the destination. What if both are present? Then the payment is malformed and Bob will need to send an error onion back. If neither are there, it's also malformed! So, again, error onion !

## The policy check: fees and timelocks

Let's assume Bob is a forwarder. Before he commits any funds, he needs to make sure this payment is actually worth forwarding. Unlike the integrity check, where the HMAC either matches or it doesn't, the policy check is measured against numbers Bob himself advertised in his `channel_update` message, and a couple of thresholds that are entirely his call.

For example, let's consider the `amt_to_forward` and `outgoing_cltv_value` fields that Bob will need to verify and which checks he will perform.

**Required by BOLT 4 (a forwarder MUST fail otherwise):**

- **Sufficient fee** (`fee_insufficient`). The incoming amount must exceed `amt_to_forward` by at least Bob's advertised fee (`fee_base_msat` plus `fee_proportional_millionths`). If Alice did chapter 2's backward math right, this will hold. If an earlier hop forwarded less than instructed, it won't!
- **Enough CLTV cushion** (`incorrect_cltv_expiry`). The incoming CLTV must exceed `outgoing_cltv_value` by at least Bob's advertised `cltv_expiry_delta`, so he has time to claim downstream before his upstream HTLC expires. To see exactly what goes wrong without this cushion, jump back to the [CLTV race-condition deep dive](/onion-routing-tutorial/pathfinding-101#cltv-race-condition) at the bottom of chapter 2.
- **Above the channel minimum** (`amount_below_minimum`). `amt_to_forward` must be at least the outgoing channel's `htlc_minimum_msat`. Whose minimum? Charlie's: when the channel was opened, Charlie set the smallest HTLC he'd accept, so Bob checks up front instead of offering Charlie an HTLC he'd just reject.

**Left to Bob's policy (BOLT 4 says he MAY fail):**

- **Not expiring too soon** (`expiry_too_soon`). BOLT 4 lets Bob reject a CLTV "unreasonably near the present," but leaves "unreasonable" up to him. Real nodes keep a comfortable buffer here, since nobody wants to race an expiring timelock. For a sense of scale, LDK fails back any HTLC within 39 blocks (about six and a half hours) of expiry, a number built from the same reorg-and-confirmation math BOLT 2 uses to recommend the 34-block `cltv_expiry_delta`.
- **Not too far out** (`expiry_too_far`). Likewise, Bob MAY reject a CLTV more than his configured maximum into the future. For instance, every major implementation enforces a standard of 2,016 blocks, about two weeks, so BOLT 4 eventually pinned `max_htlc_cltv` at exactly that.

<details>
<summary>What if Bob's downstream channel is offline or has insufficient liquidity?</summary>

If Bob's downstream channel is offline or has insufficient liquidity, then he will fail the HTLC with `temporary_channel_failure`. The <g>error onion</g> (chapter 11) returns to Alice, who tries a different route on retry. This is one of the most common failure modes for real Lightning payments, since pathfinding can't always know with certainty whether a particular channel has the liquidity in the right direction at the moment of payment.

## Forward, deliver, or reject

Once all the checks have run, Bob will decide on one of the following three actions:

- **Forward** (`short_channel_id` present, all checks passed): send the peeled `next_packet` over that channel, with `amt_to_forward` as the amount and `outgoing_cltv_value` as the timelock.
- **Deliver** (`payment_data` present, no `short_channel_id`): Bob is the destination! He compares `payment_data` against his pending invoice and either settles (revealing the <g>preimage</g>) or fails the HTLC. Invoice validation is a different protocol layer, so we won't get into it here.
- **Reject** (a check failed): Bob hop fails the HTLC and builds an error onion using the appropriate failure code and his `um` key. We'll see exactly how that works in chapter 11.
