# The Fixed-Size Packet

In chapter 5, Onion Routing 101, we saw that every onion packet is 1,366 bytes, where each field is the following size:
- **Header: 34 bytes**: 1-byte version, and 33-byte ephemeral pubkey
- **Payload: 1,300-bytes** (the encrypted routing info Alice fills with the per-hop payloads)
- **HMAC: 32-bytes**

In this chapter, we'll dig into why the fixed-size packet is a crucial security feature and how to implement it.

> Spoiler alert: This chapter will likely be a little more complicated than you're imagining!

## Why fixed size matters

Before we see how to ensure each packet has a fixed size, let's make sure our intuition is solid for why fixed-size packets are needed in the first place. Imagine that Alice goes through all of the effort to build an onion packet to privately route her payment to Dave. *However, in this scenario, there is no fixed packet size*. Instead, when each hop receives the packet, they simply pull out their payload and forward the *rest* to the next hop.

The diagram below visualizes this scenario...

<payload-shrink></payload-shrink>

Notice anything? At every hop, the packet *shrinks*. Without a fixed size, the packet is just the hop payloads and a fixed envelope (the 1-byte version, 33-byte ephemeral pubkey, and 32-byte HMAC). Therefore, Bob, the first hop, receives a 306-byte packet and peels his 60-byte hop payload off the front. Then, he forwards the next 246-byte onion packet to Charlie. Charlie peels his 80-byte payload and forwards the next 166-byte onion packet to Dave. Something seems wrong here...

<checkpoint id="cp-payload-shrink-leak-draft"></checkpoint>

Remember the privacy properties we reviewed in chapter 3? Namely, **intermediate hops should not know where they are in the path**. This rule is breaking right in front of our eyes!

## The Sphinx fix: every packet is 1,366 bytes

To ensure that each intermediate hop cannot discern where it is in the route, the Lightning Network uses the **Sphinx packet format**, which fixes the size of each onion packet at exactly **1,366 bytes** for **every hop**. For instance, in our example, the packets that Bob, Charlie, and Dave receive are all exactly 1,366 bytes.

How do we ensure this? Well, when Bob peels his hop payload off the front, he doesn't immediately turn around and send the rest of the packet downstream. Instead, he shifts the inner contents forward to fill the front gap, and then **fills the new gap at the back of the buffer** with bytes, called "padding", that *look* like more encrypted onion data.

That last part is worth stressing. The new padding can't just be *anything*! As we'll see in just a moment, if the intermediate hop were able to add anything it wanted, that would cause some pretty big issues. Instead, the padding bytes must satisfy the below criteria:

- **It has to look like real ciphertext**: The bytes must be indistinguishable from the encrypted hop payloads that came before. If they're not, then a forwarder could still tell where the "real" content ends and the "padding" begins, and we'd be right back to leaking position from the contents!
- **It has to satisfy each downstream hop's HMAC verification**: Remember, before a forwarder does anything, it recomputes the HMAC over the exact bytes it received and drops the packet if the result doesn't match the HMAC in the packet.

The second requirement is what makes this particularly hard. Bob can't just generate random-looking bytes on his own, because that would invalidate Charlie's HMAC, which Alice computed when building the onion using her shared secret with Charlie. So, where do the right bytes come from?

## Three approaches to padding

Let's compare three approaches side-by-side.

<padding-strategy></padding-strategy>

I'll admit, it's probably much easier to understand why the first two failed than it is to tell why the last one (precomputed filler) worked. Let's walk through it. 
**Remember, Alice computed each forwarder's HMAC over a** ***specific*** **sequence of bytes, and neither zeros nor random padding match that sequence!** So, once Bob peels off his payload and replaces the last 60 bytes (the gap at the back of the buffer that is exposed when Bob peels off his payload), he ***must*** replace those bytes with the exact same bytes that Alice used when she calculated the HMAC. Otherwise, the HMAC for Charlie's onion packet won't verify!

To make this work, Bob needs a way to compute the **exact same bytes** that Alice used when she derived the HMAC. But *how*? Where do those bytes come from, and how do Alice and Bob independently know how to calculate them?

To answer that, we have to look more carefully at what a forwarder actually does when peeling.

## What does Bob actually do?

The visual below steps through Bob's peel from start to finish, ending with the next 1,366-byte packet on its way to Charlie.

<forwarder-peel></forwarder-peel>

Notice what happens at step 5. Bob doesn't decrypt the existing 1,300 bytes and **then** add new filler bytes onto the end. Instead, he does it all in **one XOR pass over a 2,600-byte buffer**. Why 2,600? A forwarder doesn't know their own hop-payload size until *after* they decrypt it, so they can't know in advance how many new bytes they will need at the back. To stay safe, they pad the 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` area with 1,300 zero bytes to reach `2 × ROUTING_INFO_SIZE = 2,600` bytes and XORs the whole thing against `chacha20(rho_B, 2600)`. After the XOR, Bob slices off his hop payload from the front. This is where the magic happens! That single XOR simultaneously:

- Decrypts Bob's hop payload at the front (the first 60 bytes become plaintext).
- Peels one of Alice's encryption layers off the downstream payloads and the padding.
- Produces 60 brand-new bytes at the back, which is the exact same bytes that Alice used when calculating the HMAC.

Of those 2,600 bytes, Bob slices off his own 60-byte hop payload at the front; bytes 60 to 1,360 (exactly 1,300 bytes: the shifted-forward downstream payloads plus the 60 new filler bytes at the back) are what he forwards to Charlie.

> A note on terminology: throughout the rest of the course we'll sometimes call this whole extend-XOR-slice sequence a "shift," since the *net effect* on the buffer is a left-shift by Bob's hop-payload size (his bytes are gone from the front; the remaining content moves up; new bytes are added at the back).

## Building the filler

Before we dig into the algorithm, take a look at the below visual, which depicts each step Alice will take to compute the filler.

<filler-trace></filler-trace>

1) **Alice, at construction time**, appends 60 zero bytes (step 2), generates Bob's full 1,360-byte `rho_B` keystream (step 3), slices off just its trailing 60 bytes (step 4), and XORs the two together (step 5). That is Bob's slice of the filler.
2) **Bob, at peel time**, pads `hop_payloads` out to 2,600 bytes and XORs the whole buffer against his own `rho_B` keystream in a single pass. The bytes at positions 1,300 through 1,359 of his result (`zeros ⊕ rho_B[1,300..1,359]`) become the back of the 1,300-byte payload area he forwards to Charlie.

It is the same `zeros ⊕ rho_B[1,300..1,359]` both times, because Alice and Bob share `rho_B` (each derives it from `ss_AB`, their ECDH shared secret). **Since it's the same key, zero padding, and XOR operation, it's the same result**!

Alice repeats this for every hop and accumulates the results into one finished filler (Bob in steps 1-5, Charlie in steps 6-9), then writes it into the back of the packet as she wraps (steps 10-14). When each forwarder peels in turn, its XOR regenerates exactly those bytes, so every downstream HMAC verifies.

## The filler algorithm

For our route Alice → Bob → Charlie → Dave, Alice has to handle Bob's shift (which affects what Charlie sees as *his* last <m>s_B</m> bytes, where <m>s_B</m> is Bob's hop-payload size) and Charlie's shift (which affects what Dave sees as *his* last <m>s_C</m> bytes, where <m>s_C</m> is Charlie's hop-payload size). Dave doesn't shift anything (he's the final hop), so no filler is generated for the final hop.

**Step 2: For Bob (the first forwarder).**

First, we append <m>s_B</m> zero bytes to the filler. These are placeholders for the new bytes that Bob will produce at the back of his packet before forwarding to Charlie. Remember, by producing these bytes, he ensures the packet remains 1,366 bytes in total, since he just removed his payload, which was <m>s_B</m> bytes.

<python-snippet id="filler-append-bob"></python-snippet>

Next, Alice generates Bob's `rho` keystream, extended to cover both the original 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` area *and* <m>s_B</m> extra bytes past it (so 1,300 + <m>s_B</m> bytes total). Remember, this is because Bob will decrypt his payload and produce the new <m>s_B</m> bytes at the back in a single XOR pass. So, to produce the right bytes for Charlie's HMAC, Alice XORs the **last** <m>s_B</m> bytes of the 1,300 + <m>s_B</m> keystream into the filler.

<python-snippet id="filler-bob-xor"></python-snippet>

After this step, the `filler` contains the keystream pattern that *will* appear as the last <m>s_B</m> bytes in the `hop_payloads` area of the packet Charlie receives.

**Step 3: For Charlie (the second forwarder).**

Now that Bob is accounted for, Alice needs to append <m>s_C</m> more zero bytes to the back of the filler to account for Charlie. At this point, the filler has length <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m>: the first <m>s_B</m> bytes still hold Bob's keystream extension from step 2, and the new <m>s_C</m> zero bytes are placeholders for the <m>s_C</m> new bytes Charlie will produce at the back of his packet (which will end up as the last <m>s_C</m> bytes of Dave's view). These need to be filled in before sending to Dave!

<python-snippet id="filler-append-charlie"></python-snippet>

So, just like earlier, Alice generates Charlie's `rho` keystream extended to cover 1,300 + <m>s_C</m> bytes, and XORs the **last** <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes of that keystream into the filler. Because the filler is now <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes long, the <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes of keystream Alice just XORed in overlap Charlie's regular 1,300-byte region by <m>s_B</m> bytes, which is exactly where Bob's filler bytes will already be sitting when Charlie peels.

<python-snippet id="filler-charlie-xor"></python-snippet>

**Step 4: That's it.**

Dave is the final hop. Since he doesn't forward the packet, Alice doesn't have to add any filler bytes; there is no *next* recipient who needs to verify an HMAC.
