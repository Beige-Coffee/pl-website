# Route Blinding

Throughout this course, we've built onion packets from Alice's perspective. Alice knows the full route: Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave. She picks every hop, computes every shared secret, and wraps every layer. The onion protects Alice's privacy from intermediate nodes, and it protects intermediate nodes from each other. But there's a gap in this picture.

Dave has no privacy from Alice.

Alice knows exactly who she's paying and which node Dave is running. For a one-time coffee payment, that might be fine. But imagine Dave is a merchant who serves thousands of customers. Every customer who pays Dave learns his node ID and his position in the network. Over time, this leaks a lot of information about Dave's channels, his liquidity, and his connectivity.

## The Problem with Invoices

In the standard Lightning flow, Dave generates an invoice that includes his node ID and (often) routing hints that tell the sender how to reach him. This is the opposite of privacy. Dave is literally advertising: "Here's who I am, and here's how to find me on the network."

What if Dave could provide payment instructions without revealing his identity?

## Blinded Paths

**Route blinding** is a proposal (part of the BOLT specification updates) that lets the receiver hide the last portion of the route from the sender. Instead of exposing his real node ID, Dave constructs a **blinded path**: a sequence of encrypted hop instructions where the node IDs themselves are replaced with blinded versions that only make sense to the nodes involved.

Here's how it works. Say the full route is Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave, and Dave wants to hide the last two hops (Carol and Dave) from Alice.

1. **Dave picks an introduction point.** This is the first node in the blinded portion of the path. In our example, Carol is the introduction point. Alice can see Carol's real node ID, but everything after Carol is hidden.

2. **Dave computes blinded node IDs.** Using a blinding key and ECDH with each node's public key, Dave generates **blinded node IDs** for Carol and himself. These blinded IDs look like random public keys to anyone who doesn't know the blinding secret.

3. **Dave creates encrypted_recipient_data.** For each hop in the blinded portion, Dave encrypts a blob called `encrypted_recipient_data` that contains the real forwarding instructions: the next node's real ID, the fee, the CLTV delta. Only the intended hop can decrypt its blob.

4. **Dave includes the blinded path in his invoice.** The invoice contains: the introduction point (Carol's real ID), the blinded node IDs, and the `encrypted_recipient_data` for each blinded hop.

## How Alice Uses a Blinded Path

Alice constructs the first part of the onion normally. She knows the route from herself to the introduction point (Alice -> Bob -> Carol). For the blinded portion, she uses the blinded node IDs that Dave provided. She doesn't know who those blinded IDs correspond to, and she doesn't need to.

When the onion reaches Carol (the introduction point), Carol uses the blinding key from the onion to decrypt her `encrypted_recipient_data`. This tells Carol where to forward the payment next. Carol also uses the blinding key to "unblind" the next hop's information, allowing the blinding to chain through the path.

Each subsequent blinded hop does the same: decrypt its `encrypted_recipient_data`, learn the next hop, and forward.

## What Alice Can and Cannot See

With route blinding, Alice knows:
- The route from herself to the introduction point (Bob, Carol)
- Carol is the introduction point (she can see Carol's real node ID)

Alice does **not** know:
- How many hops exist after the introduction point
- Dave's real node ID
- Which node is the final destination

From Alice's perspective, she's sending a payment "toward Carol and then into a blinded path." The payment could end at the first blinded hop or continue through several more. She can't tell.

<details>
  <summary>Can Alice learn Dave's real node ID from the blinded path?</summary>

Not from the blinded path itself. The blinded node IDs are computed using ECDH between Dave's blinding key and each node's public key. Without knowing the blinding secret or the node's private key, Alice can't reverse the blinding. She sees random-looking public keys that she can't map back to real nodes.

However, Alice could try other approaches. If she controls nodes near Dave in the network graph, she might be able to use timing analysis or probing to narrow down candidates. Route blinding raises the bar significantly, but it's not a perfect cloak against a determined adversary with extensive network visibility. It does protect against casual observation, which covers the vast majority of real-world scenarios.
</details>

## Why This Matters

Route blinding addresses a real asymmetry in the original Lightning design. The sender has strong privacy (intermediate nodes can't determine who originated the payment), but the receiver has almost none (the sender always knows who the receiver is). Blinded paths close this gap.

This is especially important for merchants, service providers, and anyone who receives many payments. Without route blinding, every customer learns the merchant's exact node. With route blinding, customers only see an introduction point and a sequence of opaque blinded hops.
