# Peeling a Layer

Congrats! If you've made it this far, then you just implemented the code to build a <g>Sphinx</g> onion packet from a given route. Good work! Now, let's switch our perspective and learn how to peel an onion packet and get it ready to forward to the next hop. In this chapter, we'll review the process of **peeling** an onion from Bob's perspective, focusing on which bytes he extracts, what gets decrypted, and how he builds the next packet to send to Charlie. That said, in the real world, Bob will need to run some validation checks (verifying the HMAC, ensuring he is getting paid, etc.) before forwarding a packet. We'll cover those in more depth in chapter 10.

## What Bob already knows

Before we get too far, we should probably set the scene by reviewing what Bob actually knows. Since we've reviewed this payment example already from many different perspectives, it's easy to assume Bob knows more than he really does.

First, we'll imagine Bob's been running his node for a few months. He's not a novice, but he's not a pro either. He has his long-lived **node private key** (`bob_privkey`) and a channel with Alice, but that's about it. He does **not** yet have any of the per-payment keys we've been working with throughout the course:

- No `ss_AB`, the 32-byte <g>ECDH</g> shared secret with Alice for this payment.
- No `rho_B`, `mu_B`, `um_B`, or `ammag_B` (the four per-hop keys, all derived from `ss_AB`).
- No knowledge of Alice's ephemeral pubkey `E_AB`. At least, not yet!
- No idea Alice is the original sender, or that Charlie and Dave exist downstream.

At some point, Bob receives an `update_add_htlc` from Alice carrying the 1,366 bytes of onion data. At this point, it's game on, and Bob will need to derive all of the key material above. So, where does he start?

Well, with the one thing that Alice gave him: the ephemeral pubkey, <m>E_AB</m>. Remember, this is the first link in the **blinding chain** from chapter 4. With the ephemeral pubkey, Bob can perform ECDH between his node private key and <m>E_AB</m>, recovering the very same shared secret (`ss_AB`) that Alice used when she wrapped his layer. Once he recovers the shared secret, he can derive every other key he needs (`rho_B`, `mu_B`, `um_B`, `ammag_B`)!

`ss_AB = SHA256(bob_privkey · E_AB)`

## Peel, step by step

Now, the moment you've been waiting for, let's review each step and look at some code!

#### Step 1. Parse the four packet fields
First, when Bob receives the 1,366 bytes on the wire, he divides them into the following four fixed-size fields:

- **`version`** (1 byte): must be `0x00`. Any other value gets rejected.
- **`ephemeral_pubkey`** (33 bytes): the <m>E_AB</m>. This is Alice's ephemeral public key, which Bob will perform ECDH with, using his node private key.
- **`hop_payloads`** (1,300 bytes): the encrypted blob Bob will decrypt in step 4.
- **`outer_hmac`** (32 bytes): the integrity tag Bob will verify in step 3.

<python-snippet id="peel-parse"></python-snippet>

#### Step 2. Derive `mu_B` and `rho_B` from `ss_AB`
Next, Bob derives the below two keys, which he needs for verifying and decrypting the message:

- `mu_B = HMAC('mu', ss_AB)`: 32-byte HMAC key for verifying the outer HMAC tag.
- `rho_B = HMAC('rho', ss_AB)`: 32-byte ChaCha20 key for the decryption keystream.

<python-snippet id="peel-derive-keys"></python-snippet>

#### Step 3. Verify the outer HMAC
Before Bob decrypts anything, he recomputes the <g>HMAC</g> tag over the encrypted `hop_payloads` concatenated with `associated_data`. If it matches the `outer_hmac` from the packet, then Bob is able to validate the integrity of the message. If they don't match, then the packet may have been tampered with, so Bob rejects and sends back an <g>error onion</g>, which we'll cover in chapter 11.

<python-snippet id="peel-verify-hmac"></python-snippet>

#### Step 4. Extend the buffer and XOR over 2,600 bytes
Now, the fun part! Bob will append 1,300 zero bytes to the end of `hop_payloads`, making a 2,600-byte working buffer. From here, he generates a keystream using his `rho_B` key and XORs it across the full 2,600 bytes.

At this point, the front 1,300 bytes are now decrypted! Remember, since Alice performed the same XOR with the same `rho_B` key when building Bob's layer, Bob's XOR effectively strips off that layer of encryption, allowing him to read his hop payload. The trailing 1,300 bytes come out as raw <g>keystream</g>. The extension was all zeros, and XOR-ing zeros changes nothing (`0 ⊕ rho_B[1300:2600] = rho_B[1300:2600]`), so those positions hold the ChaCha20 output bytes themselves. Bob only needs the first `s_B` of them (≈60 in our example): in step 6, Charlie's `hop_payloads` is the 1,300-byte slice starting at byte `s_B`, right after Bob's own hop payload. That slice is what keeps the outgoing packet at the fixed 1,366 bytes.

So, why generate the full 1,300 if Bob only needs `s_B`? **Because Bob doesn't yet know the size of his payload, `s_B`**! Since his payload is encrypted, he won't know the size until he decrypts it, which is a chicken-and-egg problem. BOLT 4 sidesteps this by specifying that hops should generate enough keystream that any possible hop size is covered.

<python-snippet id="peel-extend-xor"></python-snippet>

#### Step 5. Read Bob's hop payload at the front
Now that the leading bytes are plaintext, Bob can parse out the relevant information:

- **`bigsize_LEN`** (1-9 bytes): the length-prefix telling him how long his TLV section is.
- **TLV records** (`LEN` bytes): any routing instructions encoded as TLV fields (`amt_to_forward`, `outgoing_cltv_value`, `short_channel_id`).
- **`next_hmac`** (32 bytes): the HMAC tag that Bob writes into the outer HMAC field of ***his outgoing packet to Charlie*** (this is `charlie_hmac`, which Alice committed to when she built Charlie's layer).

> Note, `bigsize` is BOLT 1's variable-length integer encoding. For TLV-section lengths under 253 bytes (which covers pretty much every routing TLV you'll ever see), it's just a single byte.

<python-snippet id="peel-read-payload"></python-snippet>

#### Step 6. Lift the next 1,300 bytes as Charlie's `hop_payloads`
Now that Bob has read his own hop payload, it's time to build the packet that he'll send Charlie. Note, Bob isn't inventing anything new here! When Alice built the onion, she placed Charlie's data directly behind Bob's, so the 1,300 bytes right after Bob's hop payload are the `hop_payloads` Alice built for Charlie, with Bob's encryption layer now stripped off. If Bob lifted anything other than these exact 1,300 bytes, then the HMAC that Alice computed for Charlie would fail when Charlie tries to verify it.

<python-snippet id="peel-lift-next"></python-snippet>

#### Step 7. Advance the ephemeral pubkey
This next step is pretty neat! If you remember, Bob used <m>E_AB</m> to derive his shared secret and process the packet. However, `E_AB` only works for... Bob. So, he can't simply give this to Charlie. Luckily, since Sphinx uses <g>blinding factors</g> to advance the ephemeral pubkey forward along the route, Bob can derive Charlie's ephemeral pubkey, <m>E_AC</m>, and embed it in the packet's header. Below is how he does it.

1. **Derive the blinding factor.** First, Bob computes a 32-byte scalar by hashing the ephemeral pubkey and the shared secret together: `bf_AB = SHA256(E_AB ‖ ss_AB)`.
2. **Tweak the ephemeral pubkey.** With `bf_AB` in hand, Bob performs EC scalar multiplication, tweaking `E_AB` by the blinding factor to derive Charlie's ephemeral pubkey: `E_AC = bf_AB · E_AB`.

This is the same `E_AC` Alice derived in chapter 4! Once Charlie receives it, all he has to do is perform ECDH between his node privkey and `E_AC`, and he'll recover the same `ss_AC`.

<python-snippet id="peel-advance-ephemeral"></python-snippet>

#### Step 8. Assemble the outgoing packet
Finally, Bob assembles the packet for Charlie:

- **`version`** stays `0x00`.
- **`ephemeral_pubkey`** = `E_AC`
- **`hop_payloads`** = the 1,300-byte slice from step 6.
- **`outer_hmac`** = `next_hmac` from step 5 (which equals `charlie_hmac`).

Bob hands the packet to Charlie over the BOLT 8 noise transport. Since it's 1,366 bytes, Charlie has no idea how many hops there are before or after him. That's the fixed-size privacy property doing its job!
