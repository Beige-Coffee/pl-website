# Closing Channels

We've spent a lot of time building up channels, creating commitment transactions, and routing payments with HTLCs. But what happens when two parties are done transacting? Let's explore how Lightning channels are closed.

There are two main ways to close a channel: **cooperatively** and via a **force close**. As you might guess, cooperating is the preferred option, but sometimes things don't go according to plan.

## Cooperative Closure

The simplest and cheapest way to close a channel is **cooperatively**. In a cooperative close, both parties agree that the channel should be shut down and they work together to create a clean **closing transaction**.

Here's what makes a cooperative close special:

- Both parties negotiate final balances and create a simple closing transaction.
- The closing transaction pays each party directly to a **P2WPKH** output (a standard pay-to-witness-public-key-hash address).
- There are no timelocks, no revocation paths, and no complex scripts. It's just a straightforward settlement.

Think of it like a mutual settlement. Alice and Bob shake hands, agree on who gets what, and walk away. Because there's no need for penalty mechanisms or delay periods, cooperative closes are faster and cheaper in terms of on-chain fees.

<details>
  <summary>Why are cooperative closes cheaper?</summary>

Cooperative closes produce a transaction with simple P2WPKH outputs, which are small in size. In contrast, force closes require commitment transactions with complex witness scripts (revocation paths, CSV delays, HTLC scripts), all of which take up more block space and therefore cost more in fees. Additionally, force closes may require multiple transactions (second-stage HTLC transactions), each incurring their own fees.

</details>

## Force Closure

Sometimes cooperation isn't possible. Maybe your channel partner went offline, became unresponsive, or you simply can't reach an agreement on the closing terms. In these cases, either party can initiate a **force close** by broadcasting their latest **commitment transaction** to the blockchain.

Here's how force closure works:

- One party broadcasts their most recent commitment transaction.
- The broadcaster's own output (the **to_local** output) is encumbered by the `to_self_delay` (a CSV timelock). This means they have to wait a certain number of blocks before they can spend their own funds.
- The counterparty's output (the **to_remote** output) is immediately spendable. Since the counterparty didn't initiate the force close, there's no reason to delay their access to funds.
- If there are any pending HTLCs on the commitment transaction, each one becomes its own output that must be resolved with a **second-stage transaction** (either an HTLC-timeout or HTLC-success transaction).

<details>
  <summary>Why does the broadcaster have a delay on their own output?</summary>

The CSV delay on the broadcaster's output exists as a safety mechanism. It gives the counterparty time to check whether the broadcast commitment transaction is outdated (revoked). If someone tries to cheat by broadcasting an old state, the counterparty has the duration of the `to_self_delay` to detect the fraud and broadcast a **penalty transaction** using the revocation key. This penalty transaction sweeps all funds from the cheating party.

</details>

## Bitcoin-CLI Exercises

Now let's walk through a force close scenario step by step using the **Bitcoin Node** terminal. We'll see exactly what happens on-chain when a channel is force closed while an HTLC is still pending.

### Step 1: Verify the Funding UTXO Still Exists

Before we force close, let's confirm that the channel is still open. We can do this by checking whether the **funding output** (the UTXO that locks funds into the channel) still exists and hasn't been spent.

Copy your Funding Transaction ID below and paste it into the command:

<notebook-ref storagekey="funding-txid" label="Funding Transaction ID"></notebook-ref>

**Try it in the Bitcoin Node terminal:**
```
gettxout <YOUR_FUNDING_TXID> 0
```

If you see output details (value, script, confirmations), that means the funding UTXO is still unspent and the channel is still open. If it returned `null`, that would mean someone already closed the channel by spending the funding output.

### Step 2: Publish the HTLC Commitment Transaction

Now let's force close the channel. We'll broadcast a commitment transaction that has an active HTLC on it. This simulates a scenario where one party needs to go on-chain while a payment is still in flight.

Copy your HTLC Commitment Transaction HEX below and broadcast it:

<notebook-ref storagekey="commitment-htlc-txhex" label="HTLC Commitment Transaction"></notebook-ref>

**Try it in the Bitcoin Node terminal:**
```
sendrawtransaction <YOUR_HTLC_COMMITMENT_HEX>
```

You should get back a transaction ID (txid), confirming that the commitment transaction was accepted into the mempool. Now let's mine a block so it gets confirmed:
```
mine 1
```

At this point, the funding UTXO has been spent and the channel is officially closed on-chain. The commitment transaction's outputs are now the relevant UTXOs.

### Step 3: Try to Publish the HTLC Timeout Transaction

With the commitment transaction confirmed, let's try to resolve the pending HTLC. We'll attempt to broadcast the **HTLC timeout transaction**, which allows the offerer to reclaim the HTLC funds after the timelock expires.

Copy your HTLC Timeout Transaction HEX below and try to broadcast it:

<notebook-ref storagekey="htlc-timeout-txhex" label="HTLC Timeout Transaction"></notebook-ref>

**Try it in the Bitcoin Node terminal:**
```
sendrawtransaction <YOUR_HTLC_TIMEOUT_HEX>
```

<details>
  <summary>Why did the transaction fail?</summary>

This will fail with a **non-final** error. The HTLC timeout transaction has a `locktime` set to the HTLC's `cltv_expiry` block height. The blockchain hasn't reached that height yet, so the network rejects the transaction as premature. This is exactly how timelocks are supposed to work. They prevent anyone from claiming the HTLC timeout path until the agreed-upon expiry block.

</details>

### Step 4: Mine Blocks to Satisfy the Timelock

Let's fast-forward the blockchain by mining enough blocks to pass the HTLC's `cltv_expiry` block height of **200**. Your current block height depends on how many blocks you've mined so far in the course. You can check it with `getblockcount`, then mine in batches of up to 20 blocks until you pass height 200.

**Try it in the Bitcoin Node terminal:**
```
getblockcount
```

Then mine blocks (up to 10 at a time) until you pass height 200:
```
mine 10
```

> If you're still below block 200 after the first batch, run `mine 10` again. You may need a few batches depending on where you started.

Now try broadcasting the HTLC timeout transaction again using the same HTLC Timeout HEX:

**Try it in the Bitcoin Node terminal:**
```
sendrawtransaction <YOUR_HTLC_TIMEOUT_HEX>
```

This time, the transaction should succeed. The timelock condition has been met, and the HTLC timeout transaction can now be included in a block.

### Step 5: Recap

Let's take a step back and appreciate what just happened. Everything in this channel's lifecycle, potentially dozens or even hundreds of payments, was conducted **off-chain**. Only when the force close occurred did anything need to touch the blockchain.

Here's the breakdown of on-chain transactions:

1. **Funding transaction**: Created when the channel was opened. This locked funds into the 2-of-2 multisig.
2. **Commitment transaction**: Broadcast during the force close. This settled the channel balances on-chain.
3. **HTLC timeout transaction**: A second-stage transaction, only needed because there was an unresolved HTLC at the time of the force close.

In the best case (cooperative close with no pending HTLCs), you'd only need **two on-chain transactions** for the entire channel lifecycle: the funding transaction and the closing transaction. That's the power of Lightning. You can conduct thousands of payments off-chain and only settle on-chain when the channel opens and closes.

<details>
  <summary>What about watchtowers?</summary>

In practice, not everyone can monitor the blockchain 24/7 to check for cheating attempts. That's where **watchtowers** come in. A watchtower is a third-party service that watches the blockchain on your behalf. If your channel partner broadcasts a revoked (outdated) commitment transaction, the watchtower can detect this and broadcast a **penalty transaction** using the revocation key. The penalty transaction sweeps all of the cheating party's funds, sending them to the honest party. This provides an extra layer of security for Lightning users who may not always be online.

</details>
