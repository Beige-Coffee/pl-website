# Peeling a Layer

The first half of this course was Alice's view: how to construct a Sphinx onion from a route. Now we switch sides. The next two chapters look at the same packet from a forwarder's perspective and work out what each hop has to do when an onion arrives at its node.

This chapter covers the **peeling step**: what bytes the forwarder extracts, what gets decrypted, and how the forwarder produces the packet to send to the next hop. Chapter 10 will add the validation logic and the full processing loop. For now, we're focused on the cryptographic mechanics.

## Bob's job, summarized

Bob's node is sitting around running the Lightning protocol. A peer connects, says "I want to send you an HTLC," and pushes 1,366 bytes of onion at him. Bob has to:

1. **Verify the packet's integrity** (we'll cover this in chapter 9).
2. **Decrypt his hop payload** of the encrypted body.
3. **Read his TLV instructions** (which channel, how much, what timelock).
4. **Construct the packet for the next hop** with one fewer layer of encryption.
5. **Forward** that new packet to whichever peer the TLV said to forward to.

Steps 1 and 3 are validation territory; we'll handle them next chapter. This chapter focuses on steps 2 and 4: the peel mechanics.

## What Bob already knows

Bob has his own static node private key, `bob_privkey`, that's been around since his Lightning node was provisioned. He doesn't have any keys specific to this payment, because he's never seen this payment before. Yet he has to derive a 32-byte shared secret with Alice and from that derive five named keys, including the `rho` key he'll use to decrypt his hop payload.

The cryptographic trick that makes this possible is the blinding chain we built in chapter 4. The packet's first 33 bytes after the version are an ephemeral public key <m>E_i</m> that's been advanced by every previous forwarder along the route (or is <m>E_AB</m> if Bob is the first hop). Bob runs ECDH against that pubkey with his own node private key:

```
ss_AB = SHA256(bob_privkey · E_AB)
```

This is the same <m>ss_AB</m> Alice computed when she built Bob's layer (because scalar multiplication on the curve is commutative). From the shared secret, the five keys follow.

## The naive approach: just shift and pad

OK, Bob now has his `rho` key. The first instinct for peeling is straightforward:

1. XOR the 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` field with Bob's `rho` keystream to undo the encryption.
2. Read Bob's TLV hop payload off the front (suppose it's 65 bytes).
3. Shift the remaining 1,235 bytes forward to the start of a fresh 1,300-byte buffer.
4. Pad the trailing 65 bytes with... something. Zeros, maybe?

But we already saw why zeros (or any naive padding) doesn't work back in chapter 6. The trailing bytes have to be exactly what Charlie's HMAC was computed over. Charlie's HMAC is a deterministic function of the encrypted bytes Charlie expects to see, including the trailing portion that Alice baked in via filler.

So Bob has to pad with bytes that, after Charlie's `rho` keystream XORs over them, leave exactly what Alice baked into the filler. Where do those bytes come from?

## The trick: extend Bob's keystream past 1,300 bytes

Here's the elegant move. When Alice constructed the packet, she XORed Bob's layer with Bob's `rho` keystream over a virtual region that *extended* past 1,300 bytes. The "filler" we computed in chapter 6 is exactly the cumulative effect of those extended-keystream XORs.

So when Bob peels his layer, he should mirror that operation: instead of generating a 1,300-byte keystream and XOR-ing it over a 1,300-byte buffer, he generates a **2,600-byte keystream** and XORs it over a **2,600-byte working buffer** (the original 1,300 bytes plus 1,300 bytes of zeros at the end). The first 1,300 bytes of the result undo the encryption (revealing his hop payload at the front). The trailing 1,300 bytes are Bob's keystream XORed with zeros, which is just Bob's keystream extended into the trailing region. **Those trailing keystream bytes are exactly what Alice baked into the filler at the corresponding offset.**

When Bob slides the inner contents forward (dropping his 65-byte hop payload off the front), the trailing 65 bytes of the new packet are filled by the extended keystream he just computed. Those bytes match what Charlie's HMAC was computed over. Charlie's HMAC verifies. The chain continues.

## The peel algorithm

Putting it all together, here's what Bob does:

1. **Allocate a 2,600-byte working buffer.** Write the received `hop_payloads` (1,300 bytes) into the first half. The second half is zeros.
2. **Generate `chacha20_keystream(rho_i, 2600)`.** XOR it onto the working buffer.
3. **Decode the hop payload at the front.** The first byte is a bigsize length prefix; parse it and the TLV records that follow. The 32 bytes immediately after the TLV records are `next_hmac` (the HMAC tag for the next layer).
4. **Compute Bob's hop payload size**: `hop_payload_size = bigsize_header_len + tlv_length + 32`.
5. **Lift the next packet's `hop_payloads`**: it's `working_buffer[hop_payload_size : hop_payload_size + 1300]`. The first chunk after Bob's hop payload is Charlie's view.

The next packet's `hop_payloads` is the slice of the working buffer that starts where Bob's hop payload ended and runs for 1,300 bytes. Notice that this slice naturally includes the keystream-extended trailing region: bytes `hop_payload_size + 1300 - 65` through `hop_payload_size + 1300` come from positions `1300 + 65 - hop_payload_size` through `1300 + 65` of the working buffer, which is in the zeros-XORed-with-keystream region. By construction, those bytes match the filler.

## The next ephemeral pubkey

Bob also needs to advance the blinding chain. The packet he forwards to Charlie has to carry <m>E_AC</m> in its ephemeral pubkey field, not the <m>E_AB</m> he received. Bob computes:

<m>b_AB</m> = SHA256(<m>E_AB</m> ‖ <m>ss_AB</m>)

<m>E_AC</m> = <m>b_AB</m> · <m>E_AB</m> (point multiplication)

This is the same <m>E_AC</m> Alice computed when she walked the chain forward in chapter 4. Bob arrives at it via the public ephemeral pubkey he received plus the shared secret he just derived. No coordination between Alice and Bob was needed. The math gives them the same answer from different starting points.

## Assembling the outgoing packet

The packet Bob forwards to Charlie has the same shape as every other Sphinx packet:

```
version (1 byte) || E_AC (33 bytes) || next_hop_payloads (1300 bytes) || next_hmac (32 bytes)
```

- `version` stays `0x00`.
- `E_AC` is the new ephemeral pubkey Bob just computed.
- `next_hop_payloads` is the 1,300-byte slice we extracted from the working buffer.
- `next_hmac` is the 32 bytes Bob read from his hop payload, right after his TLV payload. This is the HMAC Alice computed for Charlie back in chapter 7's wrap iteration.

Charlie receives this 1,366-byte packet. From his perspective, the version is right, the ephemeral pubkey is something he can ECDH against to get his own shared secret, the `hop_payloads` field looks like encrypted random bytes, and the HMAC is what he's about to verify with `mu_charlie`. He has no way to tell whether he received the packet directly from Alice (he's the first hop) or from a forwarder (he's downstream). That's the fixed-size privacy property holding.

<details>
<summary>What if Bob's TLV is malformed and he can't parse a length?</summary>

Bob rejects the packet and sends back an error onion. In BOLT 4 terms, this is `invalid_onion_payload`, and the response is wrapped in a return-direction encrypted error packet using Bob's `um` key. Alice unwraps it, sees that hop 0 (Bob) failed with this code, and either retries on a different route or surfaces an error to her wallet. We'll build this error path in chapter 10.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What does Bob do if he's the final hop?</summary>

For Bob to know he's the destination, his TLV payload would have to omit the `short_channel_id` (type 6) record and include a `payment_data` (type 8) record. We covered that distinction in chapter 2. If he's the destination, he skips the "advance ephemeral pubkey + forward to next peer" step and instead tries to claim the HTLC by revealing the preimage.

A nice observation: peeling happens the same way regardless of position. The destination peels exactly like a forwarder; he just doesn't forward what he peeled.

</details>
