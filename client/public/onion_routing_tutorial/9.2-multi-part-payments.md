# Multi-Part Payments

So far, every payment in this course has been a single indivisible unit. Alice constructs one onion, sends one HTLC to Bob, and the payment either succeeds or fails as a whole. This works well for small payments, but it hits a wall when the payment amount is large.

## The Capacity Problem

Every Lightning channel has a limited capacity, and the amount that can actually flow in one direction is even more constrained by the current balance distribution. If Alice wants to send 1,000,000 sats to Dave, but the largest channel along any available route can only carry 400,000 sats in the right direction, the payment fails. No single path can handle it.

This is frustrating because Alice might have plenty of total liquidity spread across multiple channels. The problem isn't that she can't afford the payment. It's that no single route can carry it.

## Splitting the Payment

**Multi-part payments (MPP)** solve this by splitting a large payment into smaller parts, each routed independently through different paths. If no single route can carry 1,000,000 sats, Alice splits it into three parts: 400,000 sats through one route, 350,000 sats through another, and 250,000 sats through a third.

Each part is a separate onion packet with its own HTLCs, traveling through its own route. From the perspective of intermediate nodes on any given route, it looks like a normal payment. They have no idea it's one piece of a larger whole.

## How MPP Works

The invoice from Dave includes two critical pieces of information for MPP:

- A **payment_secret** (32 random bytes, also called `payment_address`)
- The total invoice amount

When Alice splits the payment, she includes special data in each part's final hop TLV payload. Specifically, she uses TLV type 8 (`payment_data`), which contains:

- The **payment_secret** from Dave's invoice
- The **total_msat**: the full payment amount across all parts

Each part arrives at Dave independently. Dave sees incoming HTLCs that share the same payment hash, each carrying a `payment_data` TLV with the same `payment_secret` and `total_msat`. Dave waits. He does not reveal the preimage for any individual part.

Only when the total amount across all received parts meets or exceeds `total_msat` does Dave reveal the preimage. At that point, all parts settle atomically. The preimage propagates backward along each part's route simultaneously, and every intermediate node gets paid.

If not all parts arrive before the timeout, Dave lets the HTLCs expire and Alice can retry.

## Why payment_secret Is Necessary

You might wonder: why can't Dave just collect parts that share the same payment hash? Why add a `payment_secret`?

Without the payment secret, there's a probing attack. An intermediate node (say, Bob) knows the payment hash from the HTLC he's forwarding. He could construct his own small HTLC to Dave using that same payment hash, pretending to be "another part" of the payment. If Dave accepted parts based solely on the payment hash, Bob could trick Dave into revealing the preimage early (before all of Alice's real parts arrive), potentially allowing Bob to claim Alice's HTLC without forwarding the full amount.

The **payment_secret** prevents this. It's a 32-byte value that only Alice and Dave know (it's in the invoice, which Alice received directly from Dave). Each part must include the correct payment_secret in the final hop's TLV. An intermediate node doesn't know the payment_secret (it's encrypted inside the onion, invisible to forwarding nodes), so it can't forge a valid part.

  <summary>Why does multi-part payment require a payment_secret?</summary>

The payment_secret ensures that only the original sender can contribute parts to a multi-part payment. Without it, any node that sees the payment hash (which includes every intermediate node along the route) could send a small HTLC to the recipient with the same payment hash, pretending to be a legitimate part. The recipient might count this fake part toward the total, and if it pushes the total over the threshold, the recipient reveals the preimage. The attacker now has the preimage and can claim the real sender's HTLC without actually forwarding the funds.

The payment_secret is only known to the sender (from the invoice) and the recipient (who generated it). It's included in the encrypted final-hop TLV payload, so intermediate nodes never see it. This means only someone with the invoice can create valid payment parts. It's a simple but essential defense against probing and theft in multi-part payment flows.

## Atomic Multi-Path (AMP)

There's an alternative approach to payment splitting called **Atomic Multi-Path payments (AMP)**, sometimes called "original AMP" or "OG AMP." Unlike the MPP scheme described above (which is sometimes called "base MPP"), AMP uses a different payment hash for each part.

The idea: Alice generates a random root secret. From this root, she derives a unique preimage for each part using the part index. Each part gets its own payment hash (derived from its unique preimage), so the parts are completely unlinkable from the perspective of intermediate nodes.

Dave can reconstruct all the individual preimages from the root secret (which Alice includes in the final hop TLV), so he can claim each part independently. The atomicity comes from Alice only revealing the root secret when she's sent all parts.

AMP provides stronger privacy (parts can't be correlated by payment hash) but requires a different mechanism for atomicity. In practice, base MPP (with `payment_secret`) is more widely deployed across Lightning implementations today.

## The Bigger Picture

Multi-part payments dramatically improve the usability of the Lightning Network. Without MPP, payment success rates drop sharply as amounts increase, because finding a single route with sufficient capacity becomes harder. With MPP, Alice can leverage her full liquidity across all her channels, splitting the payment into whatever sizes the network can handle.

From a routing perspective, each part is just a normal onion-routed payment. The splitting and reassembly happen at the endpoints (Alice and Dave). The intermediate nodes are completely unaware that they're carrying one piece of a larger payment. The onion routing infrastructure we've built handles MPP without any modification. It's a testament to the layered design: the routing layer doesn't need to know about payment splitting, because that logic lives in the application layer above it.
