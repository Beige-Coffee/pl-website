## Pathfinding (BOLT 7 in one shot)

We've assumed Alice "has a route." How does she actually pick one?

Lightning nodes broadcast two kinds of gossip messages: **`channel_announcement`** (a new channel exists, here are its endpoints) and **`channel_update`** (here's the current fee policy and CLTV delta for forwarding through this channel in this direction). Every node receives these announcements and builds a local graph of who can route through whom. We touched on this back in chapter 2.

When Alice wants to pay Dave, her wallet runs a pathfinding algorithm over this graph. The standard approach is **Dijkstra's algorithm with weighted edges**, where the weight of each edge is some combination of fee, CLTV delta, capacity, and historical reliability. The output is a list of channels (with directions) from Alice to Dave that her wallet uses to construct the onion.

A few things make Lightning pathfinding harder than textbook Dijkstra:

- **Capacity is private.** Channels publish a total capacity, but not the *split* between the two endpoints. Alice can't know in advance whether a particular channel can route 100,000 sats from one specific endpoint to the other; she has to try. Sophisticated wallets use probabilistic models that update with each successful or failed attempt.
- **Reliability is unknowable up-front.** Some hops are flaky. Implementations track per-hop success rates and downweight unreliable nodes. Some wallets even let users blacklist specific nodes manually.
- **Multiple hops per channel.** A direct neighbor can be reached over multiple parallel channels with different capacities and fees. The pathfinder treats each channel as its own edge.

If pathfinding fails (no route, or every candidate route fails on retry), Alice's wallet surfaces the error to the user. The Lightning UX for "no route found" is one of the more contentious topics in the ecosystem, but it's outside the scope of this course.

## Blinded paths

Receiver privacy is asymmetric in vanilla Sphinx. The sender knows the full route, including the destination's identity. The destination, when accepting payment, doesn't know the sender (good for sender privacy), but the sender always knows the destination.

**Blinded paths** (a recent BOLT 4 addition) flip this asymmetry. The receiver can publish a "blinded route" that ends at themselves, where the intermediate hop public keys have been blinded so they don't reveal real node identities. The sender uses this blinded route as the suffix of the onion. Each blinded hop derives a special key based on the blinding factor it received and uses that to peel its layer. To anyone observing the onion, the blinded portion looks structurally similar to a normal route, but the public keys are unlinkable to real nodes.

The key idea is the same chain-of-blinding-factors construction we used for the ephemeral keys in chapter 4, but applied to node identities instead of session keys. The blinded path lets a receiver advertise a "drop-off point" without revealing which real node will actually claim the payment. Among other things, this enables payments to mobile wallets that don't want their node IDs publicly announced.

A typical use: an invoice contains a blinded path with one blinded hop in front of the real receiver. Alice has no idea who that blinded hop is; she just appends it to her route and sends. The blinded hop knows it's the introduction point and forwards using its blinding-derived keys.

## Keysend

A normal Lightning payment requires an invoice: the receiver generates a preimage, hashes it, sends Alice the hash, and Alice locks payment to that hash. Alice cannot pay without an invoice because she doesn't know the preimage in advance.

**Keysend** is a way around the invoice requirement. The sender generates a random preimage themselves, hashes it, and builds an onion that delivers both the payment hash *and* the preimage to the destination (the preimage is included in a custom TLV record in the destination's hop payload). When the destination receives the onion, it has the preimage right there. It can claim the HTLC immediately.

The tradeoff is that the destination has no proof of payment intent. They received money "out of the blue." Use cases include tipping, donations, and message-passing payments where one or both parties don't want to deal with invoice generation.

Keysend doesn't change the onion construction at all; it's just a convention for what to put in the destination's TLV payload. From the network's perspective, a keysend payment looks indistinguishable from any other.

## Multi-part payments (MPP)

A single Lightning channel can only forward a payment up to its capacity. If Alice wants to send 1,000,000 sats but the largest available route only has 600,000 sats of liquidity, she's stuck with a single-shot payment. Or she would be, without **multi-part payments**.

MPP lets Alice split a payment into multiple shards, each routed independently, all locked to the *same* payment hash. The destination accumulates shards until the total reaches the invoice amount, then reveals the preimage. Crucially, the receiver doesn't claim any individual shard until the total amount has arrived: this is enforced by the **`payment_secret`** field in the invoice (and in the type-8 `payment_data` we covered in chapter 2).

`payment_secret` is a 32-byte value the receiver chooses and includes in the invoice. Each shard's onion carries this secret in its destination payload. The receiver compares the secret across all incoming shards; if any shard has the wrong secret, it rejects the whole payment. This prevents an intermediate node from probing the receiver with a small fake shard to learn the preimage.

MPP doesn't change the onion construction either. Each shard is an independent Sphinx packet with the same payment hash and payment secret in its destination payload. The wallet on each side handles the splitting and accumulation logic.

## Trampoline routing (mention only)

One issue with regular Lightning pathfinding: the sender needs the full network graph. For a mobile wallet that only syncs occasionally, the graph might be stale. **Trampoline routing** moves pathfinding into the network: the sender picks a small set of "trampoline" nodes that have full graphs, sends them a payment with high-level instructions ("get this to Dave"), and lets them figure out the rest of the route. The trampoline node does a short hop within its known graph, then either delivers to the destination or hands off to another trampoline.

This is still in the "drafted but not universally adopted" zone of Lightning specs. Implementations vary. The principles overlap heavily with what we've built; the main difference is that part of the route is constructed by an intermediate node rather than by the original sender.

## What you've actually built

Stepping back: you've implemented a working BOLT 4 Sphinx packet builder, forwarder, and error path. With these primitives, you understand:

- How a sender turns a route into a layered encrypted onion that hides position from forwarders.
- How a forwarder peels its layer, validates integrity, and forwards to the next hop.
- How errors propagate back encrypted, identifying the failing hop without leaking position to upstream peers.

That's the routing layer of Lightning. Combined with the channel mechanics from the [payment-channels course](/lightning-tutorial) and the encrypted transport from the [Noise course](/noise-tutorial), you have a complete picture of how a Lightning payment moves from sender to receiver in private, atomic, attributable hops.

Lightning has more layers built on top of these primitives (invoices, splicing, dual-funded channels, taproot upgrades), but none of them disturb the routing math we worked through. They sit on top, using the same Sphinx packets.

## Where to go next

If you want to go deeper, the BOLT specs themselves are surprisingly readable: [github.com/lightning/bolts](https://github.com/lightning/bolts). [BOLT 4](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/04-onion-routing.md) is the onion. [BOLT 7](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/07-routing-gossip.md) is gossip and pathfinding. [BOLT 12](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/12-offer-encoding.md) is the modern offer/invoice format that includes blinded paths.

For real-world implementations, [LDK](https://github.com/lightningdevkit), [LND](https://github.com/lightningnetwork/lnd), and [Core Lightning](https://github.com/ElementsProject/lightning) all implement Sphinx in code you can read. Comparing your Python implementation to theirs is a great way to spot edge cases and corner conventions.

And if you find yourself wanting to contribute to the Lightning protocol, the BOLT-spec working group reviews proposals on the lightning-dev mailing list and via [pull requests on the bolts repo](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/pulls). The community is small enough that one well-thought-out improvement can land.
