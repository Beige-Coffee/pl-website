# Forwarding & Validation

Great, Bob can now peel a layer and produce the packet for the next hop. Is that all he needs to do? Not quite! Peeling is only part of his job. Before Bob forwards anything, he needs to verify the packet is authentic, parse his <g>TLV</g> instructions, sanity-check the forwarding parameters, and decide whether to proceed or fail.

By the end of this chapter, you'll have completed two exercises that implement the checks a forwarder runs before passing an onion packet on. The first function, `verify_hmac`, performs the integrity check. The second, `check_forward`, applies the fee and CLTV policy: it confirms the incoming HTLC pays Bob's fee and leaves him enough timelock to forward safely.

## The processing order: verify, then peel

Before Bob goes through the trouble of peeling the onion, he'll want to make sure it's legit first. In other words, he'll want to check the *integrity* of the message. How can he do that? The HMAC! If the HMAC verifies, he'll go through the rest of the steps below:

1. **Length and version checks.** If the packet isn't 1,366 bytes or the version byte isn't `0x00`, Bob will reject immediately. Why? Per BOLT 4, the only currently acceptable version is `0x00`.
2. **HMAC verification.** Now for the real integrity check. Bob recomputes `HMAC-SHA256(mu_i, hop_payloads || associated_data)` with his own `mu` key and compares it against the `hmac` field on the packet. If they don't match, then the bytes may have been tampered with, so Bob rejects with `invalid_onion_hmac`.
3. **Peel.** If the HMAC verifies, then Bob decrypts the onion. This is the chapter 9 peel we reviewed. He derives `rho`, runs the extended keystream across the buffer, and lifts his hop payload off the front.
4. **Parse the TLV payload.** Next, Bob reads the TLV records inside his freshly-decrypted hop payload to figure out what he's being asked to do. For example, is there a `short_channel_id`? Then he's a forwarder, with another hop to send to. If there is `payment_data` and no `short_channel_id`, then he's the destination, so the onion ends with him.
5. **Sanity-check fees and timelocks.** If Bob is forwarding, he checks the numbers before committing any money. For instance, does the incoming <g>HTLC</g> cover `amt_to_forward` plus his own fee? And does its <g>CLTV</g> sit far enough above `outgoing_cltv_value` to leave him his required delta? If either check fails, he won't forward, and he fails the HTLC instead.
6. **Forward or settle.** If everything checks out, Bob either hands the outgoing HTLC to the right channel (if he's a forwarder) or settles it himself (if he's the destination). If any check failed, he fails the HTLC with the appropriate error code, which, as we'll see in chapter 11, makes its way back to Alice.

## What `associated_data` is, and why it's in the HMAC

The `associated_data` is a 32-byte value bound into every hop's HMAC at construction time. For HTLC payments, it's the `payment_hash` (sometimes written `H(preimage)`). Bob already knows this value: when his upstream peer forwards an HTLC to him, the HTLC carries the `payment_hash` within it. The same `payment_hash` appears on the outbound HTLC Bob sends to Charlie, and so on, all the way to the destination.

When Alice built the onion (chapter 8), she computed every hop's HMAC over `hop_payloads || payment_hash`. When Bob verifies, he uses the *same* `payment_hash` from the HTLC. If they match, the HMAC verifies and Bob proceeds.

Why is this in the HMAC? Well, it binds the onion to a specific payment. Without it, an attacker who intercepted an in-flight onion could detach it from the original HTLC and re-attach it to a different one, getting forwarders to apply the routing instructions to the wrong payment! With `associated_data` in the HMAC, the onion is essentially locked to one specific HTLC, and any attempt to re-attach produces an HMAC failure at the first hop.

## Parsing the TLV payload

The peel step gives us back `payload_bytes`, a chunk of bigsize-prefixed TLV records. The full set of per-hop TLV types lives in BOLT 4's [Payload Format](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/04-onion-routing.md#packet-structure). There are more than we'll touch here, including blinded-path fields (`encrypted_recipient_data`, type 10; `current_path_key`, type 12) and richer final-delivery fields (`payment_metadata`, 16; `total_amount_msat`, 18). For a standard, unblinded payment, four core types do the job:

- Type 2: `amt_to_forward` (msat).
- Type 4: `outgoing_cltv_value` (block height).
- Type 6: `short_channel_id` (intermediate hops only).
- Type 8: `payment_data` (final hop only): `payment_secret` + `total_msat`.

Bob reads the TLV records in order (their type numbers have to increase as he goes) and takes whatever's present. If `short_channel_id` is there, Bob is forwarding. If `payment_data` is there but `short_channel_id` is not, Bob is the destination. Both being present is malformed; neither being present is also malformed.

For the exercises, the TLV values arrive already parsed: `check_forward` receives `amt_to_forward` and `outgoing_cltv_value` as plain integers. Parsing bigsize-prefixed TLV bytes is a BOLT 1 detail, not really an onion-routing idea, so we sidestep it and keep the exercise on the forwarding decisions instead of the byte-parsing loop.

## Fee and CLTV sanity checks

Bob's incoming HTLC carries an amount and a CLTV. The TLV in his payload says how much to forward (`amt_to_forward`) and what CLTV to put on the outgoing HTLC (`outgoing_cltv_value`). Some of what Bob checks is required by BOLT 4; some is left to his own policy.

**Required by BOLT 4 (a forwarder MUST fail otherwise):**

- **Sufficient fee** (`fee_insufficient`). The incoming amount must exceed `amt_to_forward` by at least Bob's advertised fee (`fee_base_msat` plus `fee_proportional_millionths`). If Alice did chapter 2's backward math right this holds; if an earlier hop forwarded less than instructed, it won't.
- **Enough CLTV cushion** (`incorrect_cltv_expiry`). The incoming CLTV must exceed `outgoing_cltv_value` by at least Bob's advertised `cltv_expiry_delta`, so he has time to claim downstream before his upstream HTLC expires.
- **Above the channel minimum** (`amount_below_minimum`). `amt_to_forward` must be at least the outgoing channel's `htlc_minimum_msat`.

**Left to Bob's policy (BOLT 4 says he MAY fail):**

- **Not expiring too soon** (`expiry_too_soon`). BOLT 4 lets Bob reject a CLTV "unreasonably near the present," but leaves "unreasonable" up to him. In practice nodes keep a comfortable buffer so they're never racing an expiry.
- **Not too far out** (`expiry_too_far`). Likewise, Bob MAY reject a CLTV more than his configured maximum into the future.

Our `check_forward` exercise implements the two checks at the heart of forwarding, the fee and the CLTV delta. The rest are the same idea with a node's own thresholds.

If all checks pass, Bob hands the outgoing HTLC to the channel identified by `short_channel_id`, with amount `amt_to_forward` and timelock `outgoing_cltv_value`.

For the destination (`payment_data` present), the validation is different: confirm the `payment_secret` matches the invoice and the total amount is what the receiver asked for. Invoice validation is a different protocol layer; we won't get into it here.

<details>
<summary>What if Bob's downstream channel is offline or has insufficient liquidity?</summary>

Bob fails the HTLC with `temporary_channel_failure`. The <g>error onion</g> (chapter 11) returns to Alice, who tries a different route on retry. This is one of the most common failure modes on real Lightning networks: pathfinding can't always know with certainty whether a particular channel has the liquidity in the right direction at the moment of payment, so retries are normal.

`temporary_channel_failure` is signaled by including the `UPDATE` flag (0x1000) and a `channel_update` message in the failure data, telling Alice the current state of the channel so her pathfinder can avoid stale information.

## Forward, deliver, or reject

Whatever the checks decide, Bob's outcome is one of three cases:

- **Forward** (`short_channel_id` present, all checks passed): send the peeled `next_packet` over that channel, with `amt_to_forward` as the amount and `outgoing_cltv_value` as the timelock.
- **Deliver** (`payment_data` present, no `short_channel_id`): Bob is the destination. He compares `payment_data` against his pending invoice and either settles (revealing the <g>preimage</g>) or fails the HTLC.
- **Reject** (a check failed): the hop fails the HTLC and builds an error onion using the appropriate failure code and its `um` key. (Chapter 11 shows how it's built and wrapped.)

Real implementations bundle each outcome into a small result struct, and every one of them carries the shared secret `ss` along, because the error path always needs it: either to wrap an error from this hop, or (for forwarders) to re-wrap an inbound error from downstream for the upstream peer.

## The exercises

Two exercises this chapter, each focused on a single idea.

The first, `verify_hmac`, is the defensive integrity check on its own. Compute HMAC-SHA256 over the inbound `hop_payloads || associated_data` with the forwarder's `mu` key, compare against the packet's `hmac` field, return True or False. Small, focused, and the foundation of every safe forwarder loop.

The second, `check_forward`, is the forwarder's fee and timelock validation. Given the incoming HTLC's amount and CLTV, the parsed `amt_to_forward` and `outgoing_cltv_value`, and Bob's published policy (his base and proportional fee, and his CLTV delta), it confirms two things: that the incoming amount covers `amt_to_forward` plus Bob's fee (else `fee_insufficient`), and that the incoming CLTV clears `outgoing_cltv_value` by at least his delta (else `incorrect_cltv_expiry`). It returns the BOLT 4 failure code, or nothing if the forward is safe. These are the same `channel_update` fees and timelocks Alice solved backward in chapter 2, now enforced at the hop.
