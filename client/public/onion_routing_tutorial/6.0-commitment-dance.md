# The Commitment Dance

You've built an onion packet that encodes the full route from Alice to Dave. You've seen how each hop peels a layer, extracts its forwarding instructions, and passes the packet along. But there's a critical gap we haven't addressed: how does the payment actually become *real*?

When Alice sends `update_add_htlc` to Bob, she's proposing a new HTLC. The onion packet is attached, the payment hash is included, the amount and CLTV are specified. But at this point, the HTLC is just a proposal. It's not committed to either side's commitment transaction. If Alice disconnects right now, Bob has lost nothing, and Alice has lost nothing. The HTLC simply doesn't exist yet.

For the HTLC to become enforceable on-chain, both sides need to update their commitment transactions to include it. This is where the **commitment dance** comes in: a precise sequence of messages that transitions a channel from one state to the next, with neither side ever left without a valid commitment transaction.

## The Five-Message Sequence

Let's walk through exactly what happens when Alice adds an HTLC to her channel with Bob. Five messages are exchanged:

**Step 1: Alice sends `update_add_htlc`**

This message contains the onion packet, payment hash, amount, and CLTV expiry. It proposes a new HTLC but doesn't change either side's commitment transaction yet. Think of it as Alice saying, "I'd like to add this HTLC to our channel."

**Step 2: Alice sends `commitment_signed`**

Alice signs a new commitment transaction for Bob that includes the proposed HTLC. She's saying, "Here's my signature on a new commitment for you that includes this HTLC. You can now hold a valid, signed commitment that reflects the new state."

**Step 3: Bob sends `revoke_and_ack`**

Bob has received a signed commitment transaction that includes the new HTLC. He can now safely revoke his old commitment (the one without the HTLC) by revealing its revocation secret. He's also acknowledging the new state. At this point, Bob holds a valid new commitment, and his old one is revoked.

**Step 4: Bob sends `commitment_signed`**

Now Bob returns the favor. He signs a new commitment transaction for Alice that also includes the HTLC. Alice needs this too, so both sides have matching commitments that reflect the same channel state.

**Step 5: Alice sends `revoke_and_ack`**

Alice revokes her old commitment (the one without the HTLC) and acknowledges the new state. Now both sides hold new commitments that include the HTLC, and both old commitments are revoked.

The HTLC is now **irrevocably committed**. Neither Alice nor Bob can pretend it doesn't exist. If either side broadcasts an old commitment that omits the HTLC, the other side can use the revocation secret to claim all channel funds as a penalty.

## Why This Order Matters

The ordering of these messages isn't arbitrary. It's carefully designed so that neither side is ever left without a valid commitment transaction.

Consider why **`commitment_signed` must come before `revoke_and_ack`**. Imagine the reverse: Bob revokes his old commitment first, then waits for Alice to send the new one. In that gap, Bob has no valid commitment transaction at all. If Alice disappears, Bob has revoked his old state (so he can't broadcast it) but hasn't received the new one (so he has nothing to broadcast). He's stuck.

By sending `commitment_signed` first, the recipient always has a new signed commitment *before* they revoke the old one. At every point in the sequence, both sides hold at least one valid commitment they could broadcast if the other side goes offline.

This is also why the dance has five messages rather than three. Both sides need to transition: Alice needs a new commitment signed by Bob, and Bob needs a new commitment signed by Alice. Each transition requires a "sign then revoke" pair, plus the initial `update_add_htlc` that proposes the change.

## Forwarding the Chain

Once the HTLC is committed between Alice and Bob, Bob can safely forward the payment. He peels his layer of the onion, extracts the forwarding instructions (as we covered in the previous section), and sends `update_add_htlc` to Carol with the inner onion packet. Then the same five-message commitment dance plays out between Bob and Carol.

The full sequence for a three-hop payment (Alice to Dave through Bob and Carol) is shown in the interactive diagram above. Each hop commits the HTLC before forwarding to the next. The HTLCs cascade outward from sender to receiver, locking up funds at each hop along the way. Once Dave receives the HTLC and the onion tells him he's the final destination, the fulfillment phase begins (which we'll cover in the next section).

## What If Bob Forwarded Without Committing?

This is the critical question that motivates the entire commitment dance. Suppose Bob receives `update_add_htlc` from Alice and, eager to earn routing fees, immediately forwards the HTLC to Carol without first running the commitment dance with Alice.

Now Bob is exposed. He has proposed an HTLC to Carol, and if the commitment dance with Carol completes, Bob is committed to paying Carol. But the HTLC with Alice is still just a proposal. If Alice goes offline or deliberately refuses to complete the commitment dance, Bob is stuck: he owes Carol (via a committed HTLC) but has no committed claim on Alice's funds.

This is why the protocol requires that **each hop commits the incoming HTLC before forwarding it outward**. Bob won't send `update_add_htlc` to Carol until the HTLC with Alice is irrevocably committed. This way, if Carol claims the payment (by revealing the preimage), Bob can always claim the same amount from Alice.

<details>
  <summary>Why can't Bob just forward the HTLC immediately without committing?</summary>

Because forwarding without committing creates an asymmetric risk. If Bob forwards to Carol and Carol's HTLC gets committed, Bob is on the hook for that payment. But if Bob's incoming HTLC from Alice was never committed, Alice can simply disappear. Bob would lose the amount he committed to Carol with no way to recover it from Alice. The commitment dance ensures that by the time Bob forwards, he has an irrevocable claim on Alice's funds that matches his obligation to Carol.
</details>

<details>
  <summary>What happens if Alice goes offline after sending update_add_htlc but before commitment_signed?</summary>

Nothing bad happens. The `update_add_htlc` message is just a proposal. Since Alice never sent `commitment_signed`, the HTLC was never included in either side's commitment transaction. Bob simply ignores the proposed HTLC. No funds are at risk, no state has changed. The uncommitted HTLC just evaporates. This is exactly the safety property the commitment dance provides: until the full message sequence completes, neither side has given up anything.
</details>

## Connection to Course 1

If you've completed the [Programming Lightning](https://programminglightning.com/lightning-tutorial) course, you'll recognize the machinery behind this dance: commitment transactions, revocation keys, HTLC-success and HTLC-timeout scripts. Those are the on-chain enforcement mechanisms that make the commitment dance work. Each `commitment_signed` message contains signatures for the new commitment transaction plus any HTLC outputs. Each `revoke_and_ack` reveals a per-commitment secret that lets the other side construct the revocation key for the old state.

We won't rebuild that machinery here. This course is focused on the onion routing layer: how the payment instructions travel across the network. The commitment dance is the bridge between "Bob knows what to forward" (onion routing) and "Bob can safely forward it" (channel state management). If you want the full details on how commitment transactions work, revocation keys are derived, and HTLC scripts are constructed, Course 1 covers it all.

For our purposes, the key takeaway is simple: **each hop commits before forwarding**. The onion tells each node *what* to do. The commitment dance ensures they can do it *safely*.
