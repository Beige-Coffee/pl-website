# Intercepted Traffic

Before we connect your implementation to a real server, let's take a step back and examine what happens when the Noise Protocol is implemented *incorrectly*. Specifically, we'll look at one of the most devastating mistakes in symmetric cryptography: **nonce reuse**.

Throughout this course, we've been careful about nonces. Every time you encrypted a message, you used a fresh nonce, and your `CipherState` automatically incremented the counter after each operation. But what if someone skipped that step? Let's find out.

### Why Nonces Matter

Recall that ChaCha20-Poly1305 is a **stream cipher** with built-in authentication. The "stream cipher" part means that ChaCha20 generates a pseudorandom sequence of bytes called the **keystream**, which is entirely determined by the key and the nonce. Your plaintext gets XORed with this keystream to produce the ciphertext:

`C = P ⊕ keystream(key, nonce)`

The critical point here is: same key, same nonce, same keystream. This is by design; it's what makes decryption possible (just XOR the ciphertext with the same keystream to recover the plaintext).

But it also means that if two different messages are encrypted with the same key AND the same nonce, they get XORed with the ***exact same keystream***.

### The Attack

Let's say Alice's node has a bug. Her implementation reuses a nonce, encrypting two different messages with the same key and the same nonce value. An attacker, Eve, intercepts both ciphertexts off the wire:

- `C1 = P1 ⊕ keystream`
- `C2 = P2 ⊕ keystream`

Now watch what happens when Eve XORs the two ciphertexts together:

`C1 ⊕ C2 = (P1 ⊕ keystream) ⊕ (P2 ⊕ keystream) = P1 ⊕ P2`

The keystream cancels out completely! Eve is left with the XOR of the two plaintexts. She doesn't need the key. She doesn't need to break any cryptography. The nonce reuse handed it to her.

If Eve happens to know (or can guess) one of the plaintexts, recovering the other is trivial:

`P2 = (C1 ⊕ C2) ⊕ P1`

<details>
  <summary>Is knowing one plaintext realistic?</summary>

More realistic than you might think. Many protocols have predictable message formats, fixed headers, or known handshake patterns. In Lightning, for example, the first two bytes of every transport message encode the length as a big-endian `uint16`. If Eve knows the approximate size of a message, she can guess those length bytes. Protocol-level messages like `ping`, `pong`, or `init` have well-known structures too.

Even without knowing an exact plaintext, having `P1 ⊕ P2` is already a serious leak. Statistical techniques (frequency analysis, crib dragging) can often recover both plaintexts from their XOR, especially if the messages contain ASCII text.

</details>

<details>
  <summary>Does this affect the Poly1305 authentication tag too?</summary>

Yes and no. The Poly1305 MAC is also compromised by nonce reuse. With two message-tag pairs encrypted under the same nonce, an attacker can forge valid tags for arbitrary messages. So nonce reuse doesn't just break confidentiality; it breaks authentication as well. In this exercise, we'll focus on the confidentiality side (plaintext recovery) since it's the most intuitive to demonstrate.

</details>

### Why Your Implementation Is Safe

This is exactly why nonce management is so critical, and why your implementation handles it correctly. Let's review the safeguards you've already built:

1. **Automatic nonce increment**: Your `CipherState.encrypt_message()` and `CipherState.decrypt_message()` methods increment the nonce after every operation. There is no way to accidentally reuse a nonce within a single session.

2. **Key rotation**: Every 1,000 nonce increments (500 messages), your `_maybe_rotate()` method derives a completely new key and resets the nonce to 0. Even if there were somehow a nonce collision, the key would be different.

3. **Ephemeral keys**: The handshake generates fresh ephemeral keys for every connection. Two different sessions will never share the same encryption key.

Together, these three layers of defense (automatic nonce counting, periodic key rotation, and per-session ephemeral keys) make nonce reuse essentially impossible in a correct Noise Protocol implementation.
