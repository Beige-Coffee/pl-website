# Finding a Path

Alice wants to pay Dave 50,000 sats for a Lightning-powered coffee. She has a Lightning invoice from Dave containing three critical pieces of information: a **payment hash** (which Dave generated from a secret preimage), the amount (50,000 sats), and a **min_final_cltv_expiry_delta** of 18 blocks.

There's one problem: Alice doesn't have a direct channel with Dave. She needs to find a path through the network.

## The Gossip Protocol

How does Alice know what channels exist and what fees other nodes charge? She learns this through the **gossip protocol**, a peer-to-peer system where Lightning nodes broadcast information about the network's topology.

Two types of gossip messages are particularly important:

**`channel_announcement`** tells Alice that a channel exists. It includes the two node public keys on either end of the channel and a **short_channel_id** that uniquely identifies the channel (derived from the funding transaction's block height, transaction index, and output index).

**`channel_update`** tells Alice the routing policies for one direction of a channel. Each channel has two directions, and each direction can have different policies. A channel_update includes:

- **`fee_base_msat`**: a flat fee (in millisatoshis) charged for forwarding any payment
- **`fee_proportional_millionths`**: a proportional fee (in parts per million) based on the forwarded amount
- **`cltv_expiry_delta`**: the number of blocks this node requires between its incoming and outgoing timelocks

As Alice's node listens to the network, it collects these announcements and builds a local **network graph**, a map of every channel and its routing policies.

## Alice's Network Graph

From the gossip messages she's collected, Alice discovers a path to Dave: **Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave**, using three channels:

| Channel | Short Channel ID | From | To |
|---------|-----------------|------|------|
| 1 | `700000x1x0` | Alice | Bob |
| 2 | `700000x2x0` | Bob | Carol |
| 3 | `700000x3x0` | Carol | Dave |

Each forwarding node advertises its own routing policy. Here's what Alice learns from the relevant `channel_update` messages:

**Bob's forwarding policy** (for the Bob -> Carol direction):
- `fee_base_msat` = 1,000 (1 sat flat fee)
- `fee_proportional_millionths` = 100 (0.01% of the forwarded amount)
- `cltv_expiry_delta` = 40 blocks

**Carol's forwarding policy** (for the Carol -> Dave direction):
- `fee_base_msat` = 500 (0.5 sat flat fee)
- `fee_proportional_millionths` = 50 (0.005% of the forwarded amount)
- `cltv_expiry_delta` = 30 blocks

Notice that Dave doesn't have a forwarding policy in this picture. He's the final recipient, not a forwarding node. And Alice doesn't charge herself a fee either, since she's the sender.

<details>
  <summary>What happens if Alice's network graph is outdated?</summary>

Stale routing information can lead to failed payments. For example, if a channel has been closed or a node has changed its fee policy since Alice last received an update, she might construct a route that no longer works. When this happens, the forwarding node that can't complete the route sends back an error message (encrypted in a way that only Alice can read). Alice can then update her graph and try a different route. Nodes periodically re-broadcast `channel_update` messages to keep the network informed. We'll cover error handling in detail in Section 7.
</details>

## Path Selection

In practice, Alice's node runs a **pathfinding algorithm** (typically a variant of Dijkstra's algorithm) to find the best route, optimizing for a combination of low fees, high reliability, and short paths.

For our canonical trace, Alice selects the route **Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave**. She now has everything she needs: the route, the fee policies of each forwarding node, and the invoice details from Dave.

Crucially, Alice is the one choosing this path. This is the **source routing** principle from Section 1. No intermediate node will make any routing decisions. Alice decides the entire path upfront, and she will encode the instructions for each hop inside the onion packet.
