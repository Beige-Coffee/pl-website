# The Fixed-Size Packet

In chapter 5, Onion Routing 101, we learned that every onion packet is 1,366 bytes: a 1-byte version, a 33-byte ephemeral pubkey, a 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` field (the encrypted routing info Alice fills with each per-hop payloads), and a 32-byte HMAC. In this chapter, we'll dig into why a fixed-size packet is a crucial security feature and how to implement it.

> Spoiler alert: This chapter will likely be a little more complicated than you're imagining!

## Why fixed size matters

Before we see how to ensure each packet has a fixed size, let's make sure our intuition is solid for why this is needed. Imagine that Alice goes through all of the effort to build an onion packet to privately route her payment to Dave. However, in this version, there is no fixed packet size. Instead, when each hop receives the packet, they simply pull out their payload and forward the *rest* to the next hop.

The diagram below visualizes this hypothetical. Watch the packet make its way through the route, and pay special attention to the byte count on the wire...

Notice anything? At every hop, the packet *shrinks*. Without a fixed size, the packet is just the stacked hop payloads behind a small fixed envelope (the 1-byte version, 33-byte ephemeral pubkey, and 32-byte HMAC, 66 bytes in all). Bob, the first hop, receives a 306-byte packet, peels his 60-byte hop payload off the front, and forwards a 246-byte packet to Charlie. Charlie peels his 80-byte payload and forwards 166 bytes to Dave. Something seems wrong here...

<checkpoint id="cp-payload-shrink-leak-draft"></checkpoint>

Remember the privacy properties we reviewed in chapter 3? Namely, **intermediate hops should not know where they are in the path**. This rule is breaking right in front of our eyes!

## The Sphinx fix: every packet is 1,366 bytes

To ensure that each intermediate hop cannot discern where it is in the route, the Lightning network uses the **Sphinx packet format**, which fixes the size of each onion packet at exactly **1,366 bytes** for **every hop**. In other words, in our example, the packets that Bob, Charlie, and Dave receive will all be exactly 1,366 bytes.

How do we ensure this? Well, when Bob peels his hop payload off the front, he won't immediately turn around and send the rest of the packet downstream. Instead, he'll shift the inner contents forward to fill the front gap, and **fill the new gap at the back of the buffer** (whose size matches the hop payload he just removed) with bytes that *look* like more encrypted onion data.

That last part is worth stressing. The padding can't just be *anything* that fills the gap so the packet stays 1,366 bytes. As we'll see in just a moment, if the intermediate hop were able to add anything it wanted, that would cause some issues. Instead, the padding bytes must satisfy the below criteria:

- **They have to look like real ciphertext**: The bytes must be indistinguishable from the encrypted hop payloads that came before. If they're not, then a forwarder could still tell where the "real" content ends and the "padding" begins, and we'd be right back to leaking position from the contents!
- **They have to satisfy each downstream hop's HMAC verification**: remember, before a forwarder does anything else, it recomputes the HMAC over the exact bytes it received and drops the packet if the result doesn't match the tag in the packet. Here's the rub: Alice computed every one of those tags ahead of time, while building the onion, each over one specific sequence of bytes. So a hop can't just drop arbitrary bytes into the gap. They have to be the exact bytes the next hop's HMAC was computed over, or that forwarder's check fails.

That second requirement is what makes this particularly hard. Bob can't just generate random-looking bytes on his own, because that would invalidate Charlie's HMAC, which Alice computed when building the onion using her shared secret with Charlie. So, where do the right bytes come from?

Two of these fail miserably, both for the same reason. Alice computed each forwarder's HMAC over a *specific* sequence of bytes, and neither zeros nor random padding match that sequence! Note that Bob's HMAC verifies fine: the packet he receives came directly from Alice, and Alice computed Bob's HMAC over exactly those bytes. The break shows up at Charlie. By the time the packet reaches him, Bob has peeled off his payload and replaced the last 60 bytes (the gap at the back of the buffer, 60 bytes because that matches the hop payload Bob just removed from the front) with zeros or random data to keep the packet at 1,366 bytes. When Charlie recomputes his HMAC over what he received, the result doesn't match the HMAC field Alice baked into the packet, and Charlie rejects with `invalid_onion_hmac`.

The third tab works: Alice precomputes a small block of bytes (the **filler**) that, *somehow*, results in the right last-60 bytes after Bob peels. But *how*? Where do those bytes come from, and how does Alice know what they should be?

To answer that, we have to look more carefully at what a forwarder actually does when peeling.

## What does Bob actually do?

The visual below steps through Bob's peel from start to finish. Watch him receive the packet, derive his keys, verify the HMAC, prepare an extended buffer + matching keystream, **XOR the whole thing in a single pass**, slice off his hop payload from the front, and then swap the envelope before shipping the 1,366-byte packet on to Charlie. Wow, that was a mouthful!

<forwarder-peel></forwarder-peel>

Notice what happens at step 5. Bob doesn't decrypt the existing 1,300 bytes and *then* somehow tack new bytes onto the end. He does it all in **one XOR pass over a 1,360-byte buffer** (BOLT 4 actually generates `2 × ROUTING_INFO_SIZE = 2,600` bytes of keystream for safety; the extra is just padding that gets discarded after the slice). That single XOR simultaneously:

- decrypts Bob's hop payload at the front (the first 60 bytes become plaintext),
- peels one encryption layer off the downstream payloads and the padding, and
- produces 60 brand-new bytes at the back, from `zeros ⊕ rho_B[1,300..1,359]`.

Whatever Bob's keystream produces at positions 1,300 through 1,359 is exactly what Charlie will see at the very back of his 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` field (the last 60 bytes, where 60 again equals Bob's hop-payload size). One XOR, three effects.

A note on terminology: throughout the rest of the chapter we'll sometimes call this whole extend-XOR-slice sequence a "shift," since the *net effect* on the buffer is a left-shift by Bob's hop-payload size (his bytes are gone from the front; the remaining content moves up; 60 new bytes appear at the back). The mechanism isn't a literal in-place shift, but the result is.

So filler is just Alice precomputing what Bob's extension will produce, in advance. She runs the same XOR-with-extended-keystream math at her end, so the bytes she splices into the last 60 bytes of the packet are exactly the bytes Bob's peel will produce. When Charlie verifies his HMAC, the last 60 bytes match. The math falls out at every hop.

## Building the filler

Before we formalize the algorithm, here's a step-through of Alice's filler construction. Watch the filler grow byte by byte, see how each hop's `rho` keystream contributes its slice, and then we'll wrap up by following the filler all the way into the 1,366-byte packet that Alice sends Bob.

<filler-trace></filler-trace>

The key takeaway from the visuals above: two operations produce the *exact same* 60 bytes.

1) **Alice (at construction time)**: XOR a 60-byte buffer of zeros against the last 60 bytes of `rho_B`'s extended keystream (positions 1,300–1,359, i.e. the part past `ROUTING_INFO_SIZE`), then splice the result into the last 60 bytes of the packet she ships to Bob.
2) **Bob (at peel time)**: extends `hop_payloads` with 60 zero bytes and XORs the entire 1,360-byte buffer against his own `rho_B` keystream in a single pass. The last 60 bytes of the result (`zeros ⊕ rho_B[1,300..1,359]`) become the last 60 bytes of the packet he forwards to Charlie.

These produce identical bytes because Alice and Bob share `rho_B` (derived from `ss_AB`, the ECDH shared secret between them). Same key, same zeros, same XOR, same result. Alice's "filler" is just Bob's peel-time computation done in advance.

So if Alice precomputes the cumulative XOR of every hop's extended `rho` keystream (in the right positions), she gets bytes that, after every forwarder peels in turn, leave exactly what each downstream HMAC expects.

## The filler algorithm

For our route Alice → Bob → Charlie → Dave, Alice has to handle Bob's shift (which affects what Charlie sees as *his* last <m>s_B</m> bytes, where <m>s_B</m> is Bob's hop-payload size) and Charlie's shift (which affects what Dave sees as *his* last <m>s_C</m> bytes, where <m>s_C</m> is Charlie's hop-payload size). Dave doesn't shift anything (he's the final hop), so no filler is generated for the final hop.

**Step 2: For Bob (the first forwarder).**

First, we append <m>s_B</m> zero bytes to the filler. These are placeholders for the new bytes that Bob will produce at the back of his packet before forwarding to Charlie. Remember, by producing these bytes, he ensures the packet remains 1,366 bytes in total, since he just removed his payload, which was <m>s_B</m> bytes.

<python-snippet id="filler-append-bob"></python-snippet>

Next, Alice will generate Bob's `rho` keystream, extended to cover both the original 1,300-byte `hop_payloads` area *and* <m>s_B</m> extra bytes past it (so 1,300 + <m>s_B</m> bytes total). Remember, this is because Bob will decrypt his payload and produce the new <m>s_B</m> bytes at the back in a single XOR pass. So, to produce the right bytes for Charlie's HMAC, Alice XORs the **last** <m>s_B</m> bytes of the 1,300 + <m>s_B</m> keystream into the filler.

<python-snippet id="filler-bob-xor"></python-snippet>

After this step, the `filler` contains the keystream pattern that *will* appear as the last <m>s_B</m> bytes in the `hop_payloads` area of the packet Charlie receives.

**Step 3: For Charlie (the second forwarder).**

Now that Bob is accounted for, Alice needs to append <m>s_C</m> more zero bytes to the back of the filler to account for Charlie. At this point, the filler has length <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m>: the first <m>s_B</m> bytes still hold Bob's keystream extension from step 2, and the new <m>s_C</m> zero bytes are placeholders for the <m>s_C</m> new bytes Charlie will produce at the back of his packet (which will end up as the last <m>s_C</m> bytes of Dave's view). These need to be filled in before sending to Dave!

<python-snippet id="filler-append-charlie"></python-snippet>

Then, just like earlier, Alice will generate Charlie's `rho` keystream extended to cover 1,300 + <m>s_C</m> bytes, and XOR the **last** <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes of that keystream into the filler. Because the filler is now <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes long, the <m>s_B</m> + <m>s_C</m> bytes of keystream Alice just XORed in reach back into Charlie's regular 1,300-byte region by <m>s_B</m> bytes, which is exactly where Bob's filler bytes will already be sitting when Charlie peels.

<python-snippet id="filler-charlie-xor"></python-snippet>

**Step 4: That's it.**

Dave is the final hop. Since he doesn't forward the packet, Alice won't have to add any filler bytes; there is no *next* recipient who needs to verify an HMAC.
