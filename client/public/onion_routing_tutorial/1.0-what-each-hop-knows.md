# What Each Hop Knows

Imagine Alice wants to pay Dave for coffee. They don't share a direct Lightning channel, but Alice knows a route: Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave. She can send her payment through Bob and Carol to reach Dave.

This raises a fundamental question: when Alice's payment travels through Bob and Carol to reach Dave, what does each node along the way actually learn?

The answer to this question is what separates a private payment network from a surveillance system. Let's dig in.

## Source Routing

Most of the internet uses a style of routing where each router independently decides where to send a packet next. Your data bounces from router to router, each one making its own forwarding decision, and you (the sender) have no control over the path it takes.

Lightning takes a different approach. In Lightning, the sender picks the entire path before the payment leaves. This is called **source routing**: Alice decides upfront that her payment will go through Bob, then Carol, then arrive at Dave. No intermediate node gets to make routing decisions.

This distinction matters enormously for privacy. Because Alice controls the full path, she also controls what information each node along the path receives. She has the power to protect her own privacy (and Dave's), because she's the one assembling the instructions.

#### Question: Why does source routing give the sender more control over privacy?
<details>
  <summary>Answer</summary>

When the sender chooses the full path, they also choose what information to include in each hop's instructions. In internet-style routing, each router sees the final destination (it has to, so it can decide where to forward the packet). In source routing, Alice can encrypt each hop's instructions separately, so no intermediate node ever needs to see the final destination. The sender constructs the entire packet, which means the sender decides what each node can and cannot see.
</details>

## The Privacy Problem

Let's think about what happens without any privacy protections. If Alice just sent Bob a plain message saying "forward 50,000 sats to Dave, via Carol," Bob would learn everything: who's paying, who's receiving, the total amount, and the full route. Carol would learn nearly as much. Every node in the path becomes a potential surveillance point.

This is clearly unacceptable for a payment network. You wouldn't want your bank telling every intermediary exactly who you're paying and why. The same principle applies here.

The goal is to ensure each intermediate node learns only what it absolutely needs to forward the payment, and nothing more. This is where **onion routing** comes in.

## The Sealed Envelope Analogy

Here's an analogy that captures the core idea. Imagine Alice is sending a physical letter to Dave, but she needs Bob and Carol to relay it.

First, Alice writes a letter to Dave and seals it in an envelope addressed to Dave. Then she puts that envelope inside a second envelope addressed to Carol, along with a note: "Open this, find the inner envelope, and forward it to Dave." Finally, she puts everything inside a third envelope addressed to Bob, with a note: "Open this, find the inner envelope, and forward it to Carol."

She hands the outermost envelope to Bob. Here's what happens:

1. **Bob** opens his envelope. He sees a note telling him to forward the inner envelope to Carol. He can't read anything inside the inner envelope. He doesn't know who the letter is ultimately for or what it says.
2. **Carol** opens her envelope. She sees a note telling her to forward the inner envelope to Dave. She doesn't know the letter originally came from Alice (Bob could have been the sender, for all she knows).
3. **Dave** opens his envelope and reads the letter. He knows the content, but he only knows Carol handed it to him. He has no idea Alice was the original sender.

This is the core intuition behind onion routing. Like an onion, the message has layers. Each hop peels off one layer, reads its instructions, and forwards the rest. The name comes from this layered structure: peel one layer, and there's another one underneath.

## What Each Hop Actually Knows

Now let's map this back to our Lightning payment: Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave.

**Alice** (the sender) knows everything. She picked the route, she set the amounts, and she knows the destination. She constructed all the layers.

**Bob** (the first hop) knows that Alice sent him something and that he should forward it to Carol. He does not know the final destination. He doesn't know whether Carol will forward it further or if Carol is the recipient. From Bob's perspective, the payment could have any number of remaining hops.

**Carol** (the second hop) knows that Bob sent her something and that she should forward it to Dave. She does not know that the payment originated from Alice. For all Carol knows, Bob could be the original sender. And she doesn't know whether Dave is the final destination or just another relay.

**Dave** (the final hop) knows that Carol forwarded a payment to him and that he is the intended recipient (the onion tells him so). But he does not know the payment originated from Alice. It could have come from Carol, or from some node before Carol that he can't see.

Crucially, no intermediate node knows both the sender and the receiver. Bob knows Alice is involved but doesn't know about Dave. Carol knows about Dave but doesn't know about Alice. This is the fundamental privacy property of onion routing.

#### Question: Could Bob and Carol collude to learn more about the payment?
<details>
  <summary>Answer</summary>

Yes, and this is an important limitation to understand. If Bob and Carol share information, Bob can reveal "Alice sent me a payment and I forwarded it to Carol," while Carol can reveal "Bob sent me a payment and I forwarded it to Dave." By combining this information, they can reconstruct the full path: Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave.

This is why longer routes (with more hops between colluding nodes) provide stronger privacy. It's also why Lightning implementations typically add some randomness to route selection. In practice, casual surveillance is very difficult because it requires nodes at specific positions in the route to cooperate. But a motivated adversary who controls multiple nodes in the path can potentially link sender and receiver. This is a known trade-off in onion routing systems, including Tor.
</details>

## Why This Matters

Privacy is not a nice-to-have feature of Lightning. It is a fundamental design goal. Without onion routing, every node in a payment path would see the full payment details: who is paying, who is receiving, and how much. Any node operator could build a detailed map of payment flows across the network.

Onion routing prevents this kind of passive surveillance. Each node sees only its small piece of the puzzle: who handed me this payment, and who should I hand it to next. The amounts, the endpoints, and the route structure all remain hidden from intermediate nodes.

This is what makes Lightning payments genuinely private, not just fast and cheap.

#### Question: What would a network without onion routing look like in practice?
<details>
  <summary>Answer</summary>

Without onion routing, Lightning would resemble something like the traditional banking system's wire transfer network, where intermediary banks can see the sender, receiver, and amount of every transaction they process. Any Lightning node operator could passively log every payment that flows through their node, building a graph of who pays whom. Large, well-connected routing nodes would become surveillance chokepoints. Governments or companies could run Lightning nodes specifically to harvest payment data. The "privacy advantage" of Lightning over on-chain Bitcoin transactions would essentially disappear.
</details>

## The Fixed-Size Trick

There's one more clever detail worth previewing before we move on. In Lightning's onion routing, the onion packet is always the same size: exactly **1,366 bytes**, regardless of how many hops remain in the route.

Why does this matter? If the packet got smaller at each hop (losing one layer of encryption each time), an intermediate node could measure the packet size and estimate how many layers are left. A small packet would suggest the destination is close. A large packet would suggest many hops remain. Either way, the node could infer its position in the route, which leaks information about the sender and receiver.

By padding the packet so it's always exactly 1,366 bytes, every hop sees a packet that looks identical in size. Whether you're the first hop or the last, the packet you receive is indistinguishable by size alone.
