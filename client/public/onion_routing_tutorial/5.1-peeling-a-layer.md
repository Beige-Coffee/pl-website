# Peeling a Layer

Bob has an onion packet, a payment hash, and his private key. Let's walk through exactly what he does with them.

## The Peeling Algorithm

Peeling is the mirror of wrapping. Where Alice shifted right, inserted, and encrypted, Bob verifies, decrypts, extracts, and shifts left. Here are the steps:

**Step 1: Parse the packet.** Split the 1,366 bytes into four parts: version (1 byte), ephemeral public key (33 bytes), routing info (1,300 bytes), and HMAC (32 bytes).

**Step 2: Compute the shared secret.** Perform ECDH between Bob's private key and the ephemeral public key. This produces the same shared secret that Alice computed when she wrapped Bob's layer. The math is symmetric: `Alice's_session_key * Bob's_pubkey = Bob's_privkey * Alice's_session_pubkey`.

**Step 3: Derive per-hop keys.** From the shared secret, derive the five keys (rho, mu, um, pad, ammag) using the same HMAC-SHA256 construction from Exercise 4.

**Step 4: Verify the HMAC.** Compute `HMAC-SHA256(mu_key, routing_info || payment_hash)` and compare it with the 32-byte HMAC from the packet. If they don't match, **reject the packet immediately**. Something has been tampered with, and processing should stop.

**Step 5: Decrypt.** XOR the 1,300-byte routing info with the cipher stream generated from the rho key. This removes Bob's encryption layer, revealing his plaintext payload at the front and the still-encrypted data for downstream hops behind it.

**Step 6: Extract the payload.** Read the bigsize length prefix from the first byte(s) of the decrypted data. Read that many bytes of TLV payload. This contains Bob's forwarding instructions: `amt_to_forward`, `outgoing_cltv_value`, and `short_channel_id`.

**Step 7: Extract the next HMAC.** Read the 32 bytes immediately after the payload. This is the HMAC that the next hop (Carol) will use to verify the packet.

**Step 8: Left-shift and pad.** Remove the processed bytes (payload + HMAC) from the front. Shift the remaining data to the left. Pad the right side with zeros to maintain exactly 1,300 bytes.

**Step 9: Re-blind the ephemeral key.** Compute the blinding factor: `SHA256(ephemeral_pubkey || shared_secret)`. Multiply the ephemeral public key by this factor to produce the next ephemeral key. This is the same blinding that Alice pre-computed during construction.

**Step 10: Assemble the next packet.** Combine: `version (0x00) + blinded_ephemeral_key + shifted_routing_info + next_hmac`. The result is another 1,366-byte packet, ready to forward.

<details>
  <summary>Why verify the HMAC before decrypting?</summary>

If the HMAC fails, the packet has been tampered with. Decrypting tampered data wastes computation and could potentially leak information through timing side channels. More importantly, if Bob decrypted first and then found a bad HMAC, he'd have already processed corrupted data. The "verify first, decrypt second" pattern is a standard security practice called "authenticate-then-decrypt." It ensures Bob never operates on data that hasn't been authenticated.
</details>

## Walking Through the Trace

Let's trace Bob's processing with our canonical payment. Alice sent a packet for the route Alice -> Bob -> Carol -> Dave.

Bob receives the packet and parses it:
- Ephemeral public key: Alice's session public key
- Routing info: 1,300 bytes of encrypted data
- HMAC: 32-byte authentication tag

He computes the shared secret with his private key and the ephemeral key. He derives the mu key and verifies the HMAC. It matches, so the packet is authentic.

He derives the rho key and decrypts the routing info. At the front of the decrypted data, he finds his TLV payload:
- **Type 2 (amt_to_forward)**: 50,003,000 msat
- **Type 4 (outgoing_cltv_value)**: 700,048
- **Type 6 (short_channel_id)**: 700000x2x0

These are his forwarding instructions: send 50,003,000 msat to Carol via channel 700000x2x0 with CLTV expiry 700,048. After the payload, he reads 32 bytes of HMAC for Carol.

He strips his payload and HMAC from the front, shifts the remaining bytes left, and pads with zeros on the right. He re-blinds the ephemeral key. The result is a fresh 1,366-byte packet that he forwards to Carol.

Carol and Dave will repeat this same process. When Dave peels his layer, he'll find something special.

## The Final Hop Signal

How does the last hop know it's the last? Remember that Alice initialized the HMAC as 32 zero bytes before wrapping the innermost layer. After all the encryption and decryption cancels out (thanks to the filler), Dave finds that the next HMAC is **all zeros**.

An all-zero HMAC is the sentinel value. It tells Dave: "You are the final recipient. Don't forward this packet." Instead, Dave extracts his payload, checks the payment details against the invoice, and (if everything matches) reveals the preimage to fulfill the HTLC.

<details>
  <summary>Why pad the right side with zeros after removing the payload?</summary>

When Bob removes his payload and shifts the remaining data left, the routing info would shrink. If Carol received a 1,250-byte routing info instead of 1,300 bytes, she'd know that roughly 50 bytes were consumed by the previous hop, leaking position information. By padding with zeros, Bob ensures Carol sees exactly 1,300 bytes of routing info, just like Bob did. The zeros are indistinguishable from encrypted padding after Carol applies her own decryption, so she has no way to tell where the real data ends and the padding begins.
</details>
