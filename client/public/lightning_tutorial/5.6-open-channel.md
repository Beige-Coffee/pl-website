# Opening a Channel

We now have everything we need to open a Lightning channel! Over the course of this tutorial, we've built up quite the toolkit: we derived our channel keys, constructed the 2-of-2 multisig funding script, assembled the funding transaction, built the commitment transaction structure, and finalized our first commitment (refund) transaction with proper signatures. That's a lot of work, and it's about to pay off.

In the Lightning Network, a channel is considered **"open"** once the funding transaction has been broadcast to the Bitcoin network and has received sufficient confirmations. The convention is to wait for **6 confirmations** (i.e., 6 blocks mined on top of the block containing the funding transaction) before considering the channel ready for use. This ensures the funding transaction is deeply embedded in the blockchain and is extremely unlikely to be reversed by a chain reorganization.

<details>
  <summary>Why 6 confirmations?</summary>

With each new block mined on top of your transaction, it becomes exponentially more difficult for an attacker to reorganize the chain and reverse it. After 6 confirmations, the probability of a successful reversal is astronomically low. This is a widely accepted convention across Bitcoin, not just in Lightning. By waiting for 6 confirmations, both Alice and Bob can be confident that the funding UTXO is final and their commitment transactions will be valid.

</details>

Let's walk through the process of opening our channel using the **Bitcoin Node** terminal. You can open it by clicking **TOOLS** in the top-right corner and selecting **Bitcoin Node**.

## Step 1: Get the Funding Transaction Hex

If you haven't already, go back to the **Funding Transaction** section and click the "Generate Transaction" button to create your signed funding transaction. The transaction ID and hex will be saved to your **Transactions** notebook (accessible via TOOLS in the top-right).

## Step 2: Broadcast the Funding Transaction

Open the **Bitcoin Node** terminal and broadcast the funding transaction using `sendrawtransaction`:

```
sendrawtransaction <funding_tx_hex>
```

Replace `<funding_tx_hex>` with the actual hex string you copied. If the transaction is valid, you'll see a **transaction ID (txid)** returned. This txid uniquely identifies your funding transaction on the blockchain. Make a note of it, as we'll use it in the next steps!

<details>
  <summary>What if I get an error?</summary>

If you see an error when broadcasting, it usually means the transaction hex is invalid or malformed. Double-check that you copied the entire hex string without any extra spaces or missing characters. Common issues include accidentally truncating the hex or including a newline character in the middle of the string.

</details>

## Step 3: Check the Transaction Status

Let's verify that our funding transaction was broadcast successfully. Use `getrawtransaction` with the txid that was returned in the previous step. The second argument, `true`, tells Bitcoin Core to return human-readable decoded JSON instead of the raw hex:

```
getrawtransaction <txid> true
```

You should see the full decoded transaction, including its inputs, outputs, and script details. Since the transaction is sitting in the **mempool** (the queue of unconfirmed transactions), you won't see a **confirmations** field yet. It has been broadcast, but no blocks have been mined to include it.

## Step 4: Mine 6 Blocks

Since we're on a regtest network, we need to mine blocks manually to confirm our transaction. Let's mine 6 blocks to meet the standard confirmation threshold:

```
mine 6
```

Our funding transaction will be included in the first block, and the remaining 5 blocks will build on top of it.

## Step 5: Verify 6 Confirmations

Now let's confirm that our funding transaction has the required number of confirmations. Run `getrawtransaction` again with the same txid:

```
getrawtransaction <txid> true
```

This time, you should see `"confirmations": 6` in the output. This means 6 blocks have been mined on top of the block containing our funding transaction.

Congratulations! Our channel is now **open**. The 2-of-2 multisig UTXO is confirmed on the blockchain, and both Alice and Bob hold their respective first commitment (refund) transactions. From this point forward, Alice and Bob can update their channel state off-chain by exchanging new commitment transactions, without ever needing to touch the blockchain (until they decide to close the channel, of course).

<details>
  <summary>What happens in a real Lightning implementation?</summary>

In a production Lightning node, the process is automated. Once the `funding_signed` message is exchanged and the funding transaction is broadcast, the node monitors the blockchain for confirmations. When the required confirmation depth is reached (typically 6 blocks, as specified by `minimum_depth` in the `accept_channel` message), both nodes exchange `channel_ready` messages (formerly called `funding_locked`). At that point, the channel is fully operational and payments can begin flowing.

</details>
