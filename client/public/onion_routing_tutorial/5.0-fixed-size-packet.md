# The Fixed-Size Packet

We have shared secrets per hop, five named keys derived from each one, and TLV hop payloads waiting to be packed into something that can travel across the route. Time to look at the *something*.

This chapter is about the shape of the onion packet itself: what fields it has, how big it is, and why every Sphinx packet on the network is exactly 1,366 bytes long, regardless of whether it's destined for a 1-hop or a 20-hop route. By the end of the chapter you'll know exactly what bytes a forwarder sees when an onion arrives, even though we won't actually compute the encrypted contents until chapters 6 and 7.

## The position-leak problem

Onion routing alone doesn't give you the privacy properties we want. Consider an "onion routed but variable-size" packet: each hop strips its layer, decrypting and removing its own slice, and forwards a smaller packet to the next hop.

Concretely, suppose Alice's packet starts at 2,000 bytes for a 4-hop route. Bob removes his ~500 bytes of payload and forwards 1,500 to Carol. Carol forwards 1,000 to Dave's predecessor. Dave receives 500.

This is technically onion routing. The contents are encrypted at every hop. But anyone observing the traffic, including the hops themselves, can tell exactly how many hops are left from the size of the packet. Bob sees a packet of size 2,000 and knows there are ~3 hops downstream. The hop after Bob sees 1,500 and knows there are ~2 hops downstream. The last forwarder sees 1,000 and knows it's the second-to-last hop.

That's not the privacy property we wrote down in chapter 1. We said forwarders should not be able to tell their position in the route. So we need a packet whose **size is identical at every hop**, no matter how many hops remain.

## The Sphinx fix: pad to a fixed total

The construction Lightning uses, the **Sphinx packet format**, fixes the size at exactly **1,366 bytes** for every payment, every hop, every time. A 1-hop payment uses 1,366 bytes. A 20-hop payment uses 1,366 bytes. The packet that arrives at Bob has exactly the same byte length as the packet Bob forwards to Carol.

That total breaks down into four fields:

<onion-packet-layout></onion-packet-layout>

| Bytes | Field | Purpose |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | `version` | Always `0x00` for now; lets the spec evolve. |
| 33 | `ephemeral_pubkey` | The current hop's `Eᵢ` from the blinding chain (chapter 3). |
| 1,300 | `hop_payloads` | The encrypted onion body. Holds every hop's TLV payload, plus per-hop HMACs, plus filler. Always exactly 1,300 bytes. |
| 32 | `hmac` | An HMAC tag covering the `hop_payloads` field, computed with the *next* hop's `mu` key during construction. |

The 1,300-byte hop-payloads field is where everything interesting lives. Inside it, each hop's TLV instructions are interleaved with the HMACs needed to authenticate the next hop. We'll get into the exact structure in chapters 6 and 7. For now, the takeaway is: 1,300 bytes is a hard cap on the total instruction space across all hops.

## What lives inside the 1,300 bytes

Roughly speaking, each hop's slot inside the payload area looks like:

```
[ this hop's TLV payload ][ next hop's HMAC (32 bytes) ]
```

A typical hop payload is something like 30-40 bytes (the TLV records we built in chapter 2), plus the 32-byte HMAC. Call it ~65 bytes per hop, give or take. With 1,300 bytes available, that means the maximum number of hops is roughly **20** in practice, which is more than any real Lightning route ever needs (most use 3-6 hops).

Whatever space isn't used by actual hop instructions is **filler**, deterministically generated random-looking bytes that pad the 1,300-byte field out to its full length. The filler isn't decoration: it's there so that as each hop strips its slice off the front and shifts the rest forward, the result still looks like a valid 1,300-byte packet from the next hop's point of view.

<details>
<summary>If a 5-hop payment only uses ~325 bytes of real instructions, why is the rest 'filler' instead of zeros?</summary>

Because a forwarder doesn't know whether the bytes after its own slot belong to the next hop, or to a hop further downstream, or to filler. From the forwarder's perspective, all 1,300 bytes look encrypted: their own slot is the only readable part. If Alice padded with literal zeros, here's what would go wrong: as each hop processes its slice, it XORs the entire payload area with its `rho`-derived stream cipher to reveal the inner contents. The bytes that *were* zeros would now be a deterministic ChaCha20 keystream (random-looking, but the same keystream every time for a given key). The next hop, expecting genuine random-looking encrypted bytes, would receive structured keystream output instead. The HMAC tag wouldn't match, since the bytes aren't what Alice computed the HMAC over.

So the filler isn't there to hide that there *is* padding. It's there because Alice has to precompute exactly the bytes that will end up in the trailing portion of the payload area after every layer is peeled off. We'll work through that computation in chapter 6, where it gets a chapter of its own because it's the trickiest part of the whole construction.

</details>

## Same shape, every hop

Here's the invariant the Sphinx format enforces:

- The packet is always 1,366 bytes total.
- The first 1 byte is always the version.
- The next 33 bytes are always an ephemeral public key.
- The next 1,300 bytes are always encrypted-looking hop data.
- The last 32 bytes are always an HMAC.

Bob receives a packet of this shape. After he peels his layer (decrypting, validating the HMAC, extracting his TLV payload, and shifting the inner packet forward), the packet he forwards to Carol has *exactly the same shape*. Same version byte, same field positions, same total length. The only differences are: the ephemeral pubkey is now `E₁` instead of `E₀`, the HMAC is the one Alice computed for Bob to forward (not the one he received), and the 1,300 bytes of hop data are now Carol's view of the world.

A passive eavesdropper between Bob and Carol can't tell from the bytes alone whether this is the first forward, the last forward, or anything in between. The size is fixed, the structure is fixed, and the contents are encrypted with a key only Carol can derive.

## What about really long routes?

The 20-hop limit baked into the 1,300-byte size is a deliberate tradeoff. Lightning could in principle make this configurable per packet (a 5,000-byte format would allow longer routes), but every choice adds complexity, every byte costs bandwidth, and 20 hops is more than enough for any realistic route on a network where shorter paths get picked anyway. The size was chosen to be large enough that short routes don't waste much (a 3-hop payment uses ~200 bytes of real content, plus ~1,100 bytes of filler), and small enough that very long routes get rejected by their length, not by accident.

## Where we are

We can now describe the bytes a forwarder sees:

> A 1,366-byte packet. The first byte is `0x00`. The next 33 are an ephemeral public key. The next 1,300 are encrypted, with this hop's TLV payload in the leading bytes after decryption. The last 32 are an HMAC tag.

What we *can't* yet do is build that packet. Two things stand in the way: we need to compute the filler so the trailing bytes survive each layer's decryption (chapter 6), and we need to wrap each layer in the right order so the HMAC chain works out (chapter 7). Those are the next two chapters, and together they'll close the construction loop.
