# Fulfilling the Payment

The onion has reached Dave. He peeled his layer, found an all-zero HMAC (the final hop signal), and extracted his TLV payload. The payload tells him the amount, the CLTV expiry, and the payment secret. But one critical step remains: Dave needs to verify that this payment is actually meant for him and that he can claim it.

## Dave Checks the Preimage

When Dave generated his invoice, he created a random 32-byte **preimage** and stored it locally. He then computed the **payment hash** as `SHA256(preimage)` and included it in the invoice he gave to Alice. The payment hash traveled across the network inside every `update_add_htlc` message, locking up funds at each hop. But the preimage stayed safely on Dave's node, unseen by anyone.

Now Dave checks: does the payment hash from the incoming HTLC match the hash of one of his stored preimages?

```python
import hashlib

# Dave's stored preimage (generated when he created the invoice)
preimage = bytes.fromhex("abcdef1234567890...")

# Payment hash from the incoming HTLC
payment_hash = hashlib.sha256(preimage).digest()

# Dave verifies:
assert hashlib.sha256(preimage).digest() == payment_hash
```

If the hash matches, Dave knows this payment corresponds to one of his invoices. He also verifies that the amount and CLTV from his TLV payload meet the invoice's requirements. If everything checks out, Dave is ready to claim the payment.

## Revealing the Preimage

Dave claims the payment by sending `update_fulfill_htlc` to Carol. This message contains exactly one critical piece of data: **the preimage**. That's it. No onion packet, no encrypted payload, just the raw 32-byte preimage.

Why is this sufficient? Because the preimage is the proof of payment. Anyone who holds the preimage can verify that `SHA256(preimage) == payment_hash`, and the payment hash is what locked the HTLC in the first place. By revealing the preimage, Dave is saying: "I know the secret that unlocks this HTLC. Pay me."

## The Preimage Propagates Backward

Once Carol receives the preimage from Dave, she does two things:

1. **Verifies it**: She computes `SHA256(preimage)` and checks that it matches the payment hash from her HTLC with Dave. If it matches, the HTLC is fulfilled.
2. **Forwards it**: Carol sends `update_fulfill_htlc` to Bob, including the same preimage.

Bob does the same: verifies the preimage against his HTLC with Carol, then forwards `update_fulfill_htlc` to Alice. At each hop, the commitment dance plays out in reverse, removing the HTLC from the commitment transactions and adjusting the channel balances.

The preimage propagates backward through the route. Each hop reveals the preimage to the previous hop, and each hop claims the amount it's owed. The chain of HTLCs unwinds from the destination back to the sender.

## Everyone Gets Paid

As the preimage travels backward, each node collects its fee:

- **Dave** receives 50,000,000 msat from Carol (the invoice amount)
- **Carol** receives 50,003,000 msat from Bob (Dave's amount + Carol's fee of 3,000 msat)
- **Bob** receives 50,009,000 msat from Alice (Carol's amount + Bob's fee of 6,000 msat)
- **Alice** paid 50,009,000 msat total

Alice also keeps the preimage as a **receipt**. The preimage is cryptographic proof that the payment reached Dave: no one else could have produced a preimage that hashes to the payment hash from Dave's invoice. If there's ever a dispute, Alice can present the preimage to prove she paid.

## The Preimage Is the Key to Everything

The entire Lightning payment flow, from constructing the onion to the commitment dance to error handling, revolves around one simple idea: the preimage is a secret that only the recipient knows, and revealing it proves the payment was delivered.

This is what makes HTLCs "hash time-locked contracts." The "hash" part: the payment is locked behind a hash, and only the preimage unlocks it. The "time-locked" part: if the preimage is never revealed (because something went wrong), each hop can eventually reclaim its funds after the CLTV timeout expires.

<details>
  <summary>What if Dave has the preimage but doesn't reveal it?</summary>

This is a valid concern. If Dave receives the HTLC but refuses to reveal the preimage (maybe his node crashed, or he's being uncooperative), the payment is "stuck." Carol has committed funds to Dave via an HTLC, but she can't get the preimage to claim funds from Bob.

However, the CLTV timelock protects everyone. Each HTLC has a timeout: if the preimage isn't revealed before the timeout, the HTLC expires and the funds return to the sender. Carol's HTLC with Dave will expire first (it has the lowest CLTV), then Bob's with Carol, then Alice's with Bob. The timelocks are staggered to ensure that each hop can reclaim its funds before the incoming HTLC expires.

Dave gains nothing by withholding the preimage. He doesn't get paid (the HTLC times out), and Alice can retry the payment through a different route. The worst case is a temporary lockup of funds, not a loss.
</details>

<details>
  <summary>Can the preimage be intercepted by an intermediate hop?</summary>

No. The preimage only travels backward along the payment route, and each intermediate node already knows the payment hash (it was included in the `update_add_htlc` message). Knowing the payment hash doesn't help an attacker because they'd need the preimage to claim the HTLC. The preimage is only revealed after each hop has already committed its HTLC to the next hop in the chain. By the time Bob sees the preimage (from Carol), Bob already has a committed HTLC from Alice that he can claim with that same preimage. No funds are at risk.
</details>
