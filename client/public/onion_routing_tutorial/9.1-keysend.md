# Keysend

Every payment we've built in this course follows the same flow: Dave creates an invoice containing a payment hash, Alice receives the invoice, and Alice constructs an onion to route the payment to Dave. The payment hash is what locks every HTLC along the route, and only Dave can unlock them because only Dave knows the preimage.

But what if Alice wants to send Dave money without Dave first creating an invoice?

## Spontaneous Payments

In the standard invoice flow, Dave generates a random preimage, computes `payment_hash = SHA256(preimage)`, and embeds the hash in the invoice. Alice uses that hash when constructing the HTLCs. When the payment arrives, Dave reveals the preimage to claim it.

**Keysend** (also called spontaneous payments) flips the preimage generation. Instead of Dave creating the preimage, Alice creates it herself. She generates a random 32-byte preimage, computes the payment hash, and uses that hash for the HTLCs along the route, just like a normal payment.

The trick is: Alice needs to get the preimage to Dave so he can claim the payment. She does this by tucking the preimage into Dave's hop payload as a custom **TLV record**.

## How Keysend Works

Here's the step-by-step:

1. **Alice generates a preimage.** She picks a random 32 bytes and computes `payment_hash = SHA256(preimage)`.

2. **Alice constructs the onion normally.** She builds the route, computes shared secrets, and wraps the onion layer by layer. The HTLCs along the route all use her payment hash.

3. **Alice includes the preimage in Dave's TLV payload.** In Dave's (final hop) payload, Alice adds a custom TLV record with type **5482373484**. The value is the 32-byte preimage.

4. **The payment routes normally.** Bob and Carol forward the HTLCs as usual. They don't know or care that this is a keysend payment. The onion and HTLCs look identical to any other payment.

5. **Dave receives the payment.** He peels his onion layer and finds the preimage in the custom TLV record. He computes `SHA256(preimage)` and verifies it matches the payment hash from the incoming HTLC. If it matches, he can claim the payment by revealing the preimage backward through the route.

From the perspective of intermediate nodes, a keysend payment is indistinguishable from an invoice-based payment. The difference is entirely between Alice and Dave.

## The Tradeoff

Keysend is convenient, but it comes with a significant tradeoff compared to invoice-based payments.

With an invoice, Dave explicitly requested the payment. He chose the preimage, he set the amount, and he distributed the invoice. The existence of the invoice is proof that Dave intended to receive this payment. And when Alice obtains the preimage (by completing the payment), she has cryptographic proof that Dave was paid, because only the person who created the payment hash could know the preimage.

With keysend, none of that exists. Dave never requested the payment. Alice generated the preimage herself, so she already had it before the payment was sent. The preimage doesn't prove anything about Dave's intent or even that Dave received the funds (Alice could have generated the preimage without sending any payment at all).

  <summary>What's the security tradeoff of keysend vs. invoice-based payments?</summary>

The core tradeoff is **proof of payment**. In an invoice-based payment, the preimage serves as a receipt: Alice can prove she paid Dave because only Dave could have revealed the preimage that matches the payment hash in Dave's invoice. The invoice (signed by Dave) proves Dave requested the payment, and the preimage proves Alice fulfilled it.

With keysend, Alice generated the preimage herself. She can't use it as proof of payment because she had it all along. There's no invoice from Dave proving he requested anything. If Alice claims "I sent Dave 50,000 sats via keysend," Dave could deny it, and there's no cryptographic evidence to settle the dispute.

This makes keysend unsuitable for situations where proof of payment matters (commerce, contracts, settling debts). But for use cases where proof isn't needed (tipping a content creator, donating to a cause, pushing funds between your own nodes), keysend is simpler because it requires no prior coordination with the recipient.

## Use Cases

Despite the tradeoff, keysend is widely used in the Lightning ecosystem:

- **Tipping.** Podcast apps use keysend to stream sats to creators while listeners play episodes. The listener's app sends tiny keysend payments to the creator's node without requiring invoices.
- **Donations.** A node operator can publish their node ID and receive donations from anyone, without needing to generate a unique invoice for each donor.
- **Push payments.** If you run multiple nodes, you can push liquidity between them without the receiving node needing to create invoices.

Keysend demonstrates something elegant about the onion routing system we've built. The TLV payload format is flexible enough to carry arbitrary data to the final hop, and the intermediate nodes are completely unaware of what's inside. Alice just adds one extra TLV field, and the entire existing routing infrastructure handles the rest.
