# Peeling a Layer

The first half of this course was Alice's view: how to construct a Sphinx onion from a route. Now we switch sides. The next two chapters look at the same packet from a forwarder's perspective, working out what each hop has to do when an onion arrives.

This chapter covers the peeling step: what bytes are extracted, what gets decrypted, and how the forwarder produces the packet for the next hop. Chapter 9 will add the validation logic and the full processing loop.

## The forwarder's job, summarized

Bob's node is sitting around running the Lightning protocol. A peer connects, says "I want to send you an HTLC," and pushes 1,366 bytes of onion at him. Bob has to:

1. **Verify the packet's integrity** before processing any of its contents.
2. **Decrypt his slice** of the encrypted body.
3. **Read his TLV instructions** (which channel, how much, what timelock).
4. **Construct the packet for the next hop** with one fewer layer of encryption.
5. **Forward** that new packet to whichever peer the TLV said to forward to.

Validation (step 1) and reading instructions (step 3) we'll handle in detail in chapter 9. This chapter focuses on steps 2 and 4: peeling the layer and producing the outgoing packet.

## What Bob already knows when an onion arrives

Bob has his own static node private key, `bob_privkey`, that's been around since his Lightning node was provisioned. He doesn't have any keys specific to this payment, because he's never seen this payment before. Yet he has to derive a 32-byte shared secret with Alice and from that derive five named keys, including the `rho` key he'll use to decrypt his slice.

The cryptographic trick that makes this possible is the blinding chain we built in chapter 3. The packet's first 33 bytes after the version are an ephemeral public key `E_i` that's been advanced by every previous forwarder along the route (or is `E_0` if Bob is the first hop). Bob runs his ECDH against that pubkey and gets exactly the same shared secret Alice computed when she built his layer. From the shared secret, the five keys follow.

## The peel operation

Once Bob has `rho_i`, peeling is conceptually a single XOR. The hop_payloads field on the wire is encrypted; XOR with `rho_i`-derived keystream and you get the plaintext. But there's a subtlety: when Bob shifts his slice off the front and forwards a 1,300-byte buffer, the trailing bytes of that buffer have to come from *somewhere*. The clever Sphinx trick is that those trailing bytes are part of an **extended keystream** that goes past the 1,300-byte boundary.

Here's the full peel algorithm:

1. Allocate a 2,600-byte working buffer. Write the received `hop_payloads` (1,300 bytes) into the first half. The second half is zeros.
2. Generate `chacha20_keystream(rho_i, 2600)`. XOR it onto the working buffer.
3. The first bytes of the buffer are now Bob's TLV payload, followed by `next_hmac`, followed by what would be Carol's view of the hop_payloads.
4. Parse the TLV to determine `payload_size`. Read `next_hmac` from offset `payload_size`. The slot Bob occupied is `slot_size = payload_size + 32` bytes.
5. The next packet's hop_payloads field is `working_buffer[slot_size : slot_size + 1300]`. (We lift it from the decrypted-plus-keystream-extended buffer, sliding the window forward by `slot_size`.)

Why is the keystream extended past 1,300 bytes? Because when Bob shifts the inner contents forward, the gap that opens at the end has to be filled with bytes that are still encrypted from Carol's perspective. The trailing portion of Bob's `rho` keystream is exactly those bytes: from Carol's point of view, the bytes Bob shifted in were XORed with Bob's `rho` keystream during construction, and Bob applies that same keystream when he shifts. So Carol's HMAC verification will see a buffer whose trailing bytes match what Alice baked in via filler back in chapter 6.

This is the same idea we approached from the other side: the filler exists to make this peel-and-shift operation produce a buffer that Carol can verify. Bob's job is just to apply the operation; the math was already done by Alice.

## The next ephemeral pubkey

Bob also needs to advance the blinding chain. The packet he forwards to Carol must have `E_{i+1}` in its ephemeral pubkey field, not the `E_i` he received. Bob computes:

```
b_i = SHA256(E_i || ss_i)
E_{i+1} = E_i * b_i      (point multiplication)
```

This is the same `E_{i+1}` Alice computed when she walked the chain forward in chapter 3. Bob arrived at it via the public ephemeral pubkey he received plus the shared secret he just derived. No coordination between Alice and Bob was needed; the math gives them the same answer from different starting points.

## Assembling the outgoing packet

The packet Bob forwards to Carol has the same shape as every other Sphinx packet:

```
version (1 byte) || E_{i+1} (33 bytes) || next_hop_payloads (1300 bytes) || next_hmac (32 bytes)
```

- `version` stays `0x00`.
- `E_{i+1}` is the new ephemeral pubkey Bob just computed.
- `next_hop_payloads` is the 1,300-byte slice we extracted from the working buffer (the bytes after Bob's slot).
- `next_hmac` is the 32 bytes Bob read from his slot, right after his TLV payload. This is the HMAC Alice computed for Carol back in chapter 7's wrap_hop iteration.

Carol receives this 1,366-byte packet. From her perspective, the version is right, the ephemeral pubkey is something she can ECDH against to get her own shared secret, the hop_payloads field looks like encrypted random bytes, and the HMAC is what she's about to verify with `mu_carol`. She has no way to tell whether she received the packet directly from Alice (she's the first hop) or from a forwarder (she's downstream). That's the privacy property holding.

<details>
<summary>What if Bob's TLV is malformed and he can't parse a length?</summary>

Bob rejects the packet and sends back an error onion. In BOLT 4 terms, this is `invalid_onion_payload` and the response is wrapped in a return-direction encrypted error packet using Bob's `um` key. Alice unwraps it, sees that hop 0 (Bob) failed with this code, and either retries on a different route or surfaces an error to her wallet. We'll build this error path in chapter 10.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What does Bob do if he's the final hop?</summary>

For Bob to know he's the destination, his TLV payload would have to omit the `short_channel_id` (type 6) record. We covered that in chapter 2: the absence of type 6 signals "you are Dave." If that's the case, Bob would skip the "advance ephemeral pubkey + forward to next peer" step and instead try to claim the HTLC by revealing the preimage. For our purposes here (Bob is a forwarder, not a destination), we'll assume `short_channel_id` is present.

A nice observation: peeling happens the same way regardless of position. The destination peels exactly like a forwarder; it just doesn't forward what it peels.

</details>
