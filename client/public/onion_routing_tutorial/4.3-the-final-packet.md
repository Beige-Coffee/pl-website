# The Final Packet

You've just constructed a complete onion packet. Let's take a step back and look at what Alice has built.

## The 1,366-Byte Packet

The onion packet that Alice includes in the `update_add_htlc` message to Bob is exactly **1,366 bytes**:

<final-packet></final-packet>

- **Version** (`0x00`): A single byte identifying the packet format. Currently always zero.
- **Ephemeral Public Key** (33 bytes): The session public key that Alice generated for this payment. This is the first key in the ephemeral key chain. Bob will use it to compute the shared secret via ECDH.
- **Encrypted Payload** (1,300 bytes): The layered, encrypted routing data. Contains TLV payloads for every hop, wrapped in nested XOR encryption. Fixed size regardless of route length.
- **HMAC** (32 bytes): The outermost authentication tag. Bob verifies this before touching anything else. If it doesn't match, the packet is rejected immediately.

## What Happens Next

When Bob receives this packet (along with the HTLC details), he performs the reverse of what Alice did:

1. **Verify**: Compute the shared secret from the ephemeral public key and his private key. Derive the `mu` key. Verify the HMAC over the encrypted payload.
2. **Decrypt**: Derive the `rho` key. Generate the cipher stream. XOR-decrypt the 1,300-byte payload.
3. **Extract**: Read his TLV payload from the front of the decrypted data. Read the next 32 bytes as Carol's HMAC.
4. **Forward**: The remaining bytes become Carol's encrypted payload. Compute the blinded ephemeral public key for Carol. Assemble a new 1,366-byte packet and forward it.

Carol does the same thing, passing the packet to Dave. Dave finds an all-zero HMAC, which signals that he's the final destination. He extracts the payment details and fulfills the HTLC.

<details>
  <summary>How does Dave know he's the last hop?</summary>

During construction, Alice initialized the HMAC as 32 zero bytes before wrapping Dave's layer. After all the encryption and decryption cancels out (thanks to the filler and layered XOR), Dave finds this all-zero HMAC when he peels his layer. An all-zero HMAC is the sentinel value that means "you are the final recipient." No further forwarding is needed.
</details>

## The Privacy Properties

Let's revisit the three problems from our naive approach and confirm they're solved:

**Fixed size**: Every hop receives and forwards a 1,366-byte packet. Bob can't tell whether he's the first hop or the tenth. The packet looks the same size to everyone.

**Layered encryption**: Each layer of XOR encryption wraps all inner layers. Bob can only decrypt one layer (his own). After decryption, he sees Carol's encrypted layer, not the raw structure underneath.

**Authentication**: The HMAC at each layer verifies the entire encrypted payload. If any byte has been tampered with, the HMAC check fails and the packet is rejected before any further processing.
