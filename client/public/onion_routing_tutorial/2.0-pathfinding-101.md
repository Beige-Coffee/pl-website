# Pathfinding 101

In the last section, we saw Alice send a 10,000 sat <g>HTLC</g> through Bob → Charlie → Dave. But how did Alice know that was the ideal path?

To answer that question, it's helpful to start by reviewing how nodes on the Lightning Network learn about each other's presence and preferences for forwarding payments.

## Building a Lightning Network Graph

When Alice first joins the Lightning Network, she likely won't have a very clear view of the network. Since Lightning is decentralized, there is no central authority that she can ping to fetch a robust and complete view. Instead, she will have to join the peer-to-peer **gossip protocol**, which defines how Lightning nodes should advertise their payment channels, which features they support, and their fee structure.

The full list of gossip messages can be found in [BOLT 7](https://github.com/lightning/bolts/blob/master/07-routing-gossip.md). For this course, we'll focus on the following:
- **`channel_announcement`**: Published when a channel opens. This message points to the on-chain 2-of-2 multisig output and provides both the bitcoin public keys and signatures, enabling other nodes on the network to verify that the publisher of the message truly owns the private key to the on-chain Funding Transaction.
- **`channel_update`**: Published whenever a forwarder sets or changes its forwarding policy and fee structure. Each `channel_update` is signed, and carries several fields that help peers update their graph and evaluate whether the channel is worth using to send a payment:
  - **`short_channel_id`**: Identifier for the channel.
  - **`timestamp`**: Lets peers keep only the most recent update per channel.
  - **`chain_hash`**: Tells peers which network (mainnet, testnet, etc.) this update belongs to.
  - **`channel_flags`**: Indicates which side of the channel is publishing this update.
  - **`htlc_minimum_msat`** / **`htlc_maximum_msat`**: The smallest and largest HTLCs this forwarder is willing to relay.
  - **`fee_base_msat`** (often shortened to `base_fee`): A flat fee paid for every forwarded HTLC regardless of size.
  - **`fee_proportional_millionths`** (often shortened to `ppm` or `fee_rate`): A proportional fee, in parts per million of the forwarded amount.
  - **`cltv_expiry_delta`**: The number of blocks of safety margin the forwarder requires between the timelock on its *incoming* HTLC and the timelock on its *outgoing* HTLC. This buffer gives the forwarder enough time to claim its incoming HTLC on-chain if the downstream hop stalls or only reveals the <g>preimage</g> at the very last second. If you want to see exactly what goes wrong without this margin, jump to the [optional deep dive on the CLTV race condition](#cltv-race-condition) at the bottom of this chapter!

For the rest of this chapter we'll focus on the three fields that are both important for pathfinding and understanding Lightning payments: `fee_base_msat`, `fee_proportional_millionths`, and `cltv_expiry_delta`.

## Choosing a payment path

As you can see, Alice has multiple potential paths to choose from. To decide which is "best", Alice will use a **pathfinding algorithm**.

Every major Lightning implementation runs a variant of **Dijkstra's algorithm**, a classic shortest-path search, over the gossip graph. Each one weights channels a little differently, but they all blend the channel's fee with an estimate of how likely the payment is to actually succeed. The links below dig into each major implementation's pathfinding approach.

- **[LND](https://docs.lightning.engineering/lightning-network-tools/lnd/pathfinding)**
- **[Core Lightning](https://docs.corelightning.org/reference/renepay)**
- **[LDK](https://lightningdevkit.org/blog/ldk-pathfinding/)**
- **[Eclair](https://github.com/ACINQ/eclair)**

For the rest of this chapter, we'll build our intuition for how fees and timelocks impact pathfinding by calculating the total fees and timelocks required for each of the three paths above.

## The fee formula

Now that Alice has each node's fee policy in hand, she can compute how much it will cost to route across each hop in the path. To calculate the cost to forward a payment across a given channel, Alice can use the following formula:

```
fee = base_fee + (fee_per_millionth / 1,000,000) × amount_forwarded
```

So a forwarder with `base_fee = 100`, `fee_per_millionth = 3,000`, forwarding 400,000 sats will charge:

```
100 + (3,000 / 1,000,000) × 400,000 = 100 + 1,200 = 1,300 sats
```

Alice has to pay this fee **in addition to** the actual payment amount. These fees are what compensate Lightning operators for tying up their liquidity while the payment is in flight. Without fees, the Lightning Network would rely on altruism for forwarding payments, which, while generous, is not a robust incentive mechanism!

## Optional deep dive: the CLTV race condition

<details>
<summary>How forwarders avoid getting burned by stalls and last-second preimage reveals</summary>

A nuanced thinker might object to the atomicity argument: "but what if an intermediary node doesn't cooperate and, instead, closes the channel on-chain or only reveals the preimage at the very last second?" Great question, and this is the reason why the contract at the final hop is set to expire *first*, with each upstream contract expiring later in reverse order. This way, if any intermediary hop closes their channel on-chain or stalls until the very last second to reveal the preimage, their counterparty still has time to grab the preimage and claim funds from *their* incoming HTLC.

For instance, in our example above, imagine all contracts expire at the same time and Charlie waits until the last second to reveal the preimage. Charlie uses it to claim Bob's HTLC, but Bob may not have enough time to turn around and claim his HTLC from Alice before her timelock fires. Alice would claw her funds back via timeout, *and* Dave would still have been paid (since Charlie already paid him). Bob is left out of pocket. The reverse-ordered expiries exist precisely to prevent this.
