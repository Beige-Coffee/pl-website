# The Privacy Problem

Picture a Lightning payment in motion. **Alice** wants to pay 10,000 sats to **Dave**, but Alice and Dave don't share a channel. They have a couple of friends in common: **Bob** has a channel with both Alice and Carol, and **Carol** has a channel with both Bob and Dave. So the payment travels Alice → Bob → Carol → Dave, hopping across one channel at a time.

That works. The money gets there. But before we explain *how* it gets there, we need to ask a question that turns out to shape the entire design: **what does each person learn along the way?**

## Why privacy matters here

In a normal Bitcoin transaction, the whole world can see the sender's address, the receiver's address, and the amount. Lightning was designed in part to fix that. Payments don't go on chain, so the public ledger doesn't see them. That's a great start, but it leaves a different problem: the people who *do* help carry the payment.

Bob and Carol aren't the public. They're real Lightning nodes run by real people. Each one is a potential observer. If Bob can read who's paying whom, Bob now knows something pretty intimate. If Carol can read it too, the privacy story collapses to "your privacy depends on every node along the route being well-behaved." That's not the tradeoff we want.

We want something stronger: forwarders should know *just enough to do their job, and nothing more.* If we can pull that off, then even a curious or compromised forwarder learns almost nothing. The information they'd need to deanonymize the payment is missing from what they see.

## Payments are atomic (or they don't happen at all)

Before we go any further, there's a property we need to nail down because everything else in the course rests on it: a Lightning payment is **atomic**. Either every hop in the route forwards successfully and Dave gets paid, or none of them keep any money and the whole thing unwinds. There's no middle ground where Bob ends up with sats he wasn't supposed to keep, or Carol forwards but Dave never receives.

Lightning gets this property from **HTLCs** (Hash Time-Locked Contracts), which we covered in the payment-channels course. The short version: each hop's HTLC is conditional on revealing a secret called the **preimage**. Dave is the only one who knows the preimage when the payment starts, because he generated it (or his invoice-issuing wallet did). When the onion arrives at Dave, he reveals the preimage to claim Carol's HTLC. Carol then uses that same preimage to claim Bob's HTLC. Bob uses it to claim Alice's. The preimage propagates backward, and every hop settles in turn. If anything goes wrong before Dave reveals the preimage, every HTLC times out and the funds return to wherever they came from.

That atomicity is what makes "what does each hop learn" the right question to ask. Without it, we'd also have to ask "what if Bob keeps the money and refuses to forward?" or "what if Carol forwards but Dave never confirms?" Those failure modes don't exist on Lightning because the protocol is structured so they can't.

So when we say Alice's onion has to deliver instructions to every hop intact, we mean it: the cryptographic construction we're about to build only works if the payment reaches Dave. Partial onion-routing isn't a thing.

## The naive design (and why it leaks)

Before we get to the real construction, let's spend a minute on a design that would be tempting to build first, and watch it fall over. This is the "gently introduce" approach we'll keep using throughout the course: see why a simple version doesn't work, then motivate the real one.

Imagine Alice sticks a header on her payment that just lists the route in plaintext:

> ```
> Sender:  Alice
> Hop 1:   Bob   (forward 10,010 sats, expire at block 220)
> Hop 2:   Carol (forward 10,005 sats, expire at block 180)
> Hop 3:   Dave  (final, 10,000 sats, expire at block 140)
> ```

She hands it to Bob. Bob reads it, takes his fee, forwards what's left to Carol. Carol does the same. Dave receives the final amount.

This is dead simple. It also leaks everything. Bob can see Alice initiated the payment. Bob can see Dave is the destination. Bob can compute everyone's fee. Carol can do the same. So can a passive eavesdropper sniffing Alice's connection.

We didn't even try to be private. We just built the obvious thing. So we know that "the obvious thing" isn't enough. Privacy has to be designed in.

## The properties we want

Let's write down what we'd ideally like each participant to know after the payment is complete.

**The sender (Alice):** Has to know the full route. She's the one constructing the payment, so this is unavoidable.

**A forwarder (Bob, Carol):** Knows only:
- The hop *before* it, because that's who sent the bytes that just arrived.
- The hop *after* it, because that's who it needs to forward to.
- The amount it should forward, and when the timelock expires.

A forwarder does **not** know:
- Who started the payment.
- Who finishes the payment.
- How long the route is.
- Where in the route it sits (first hop? last forwarder? middle?).
- Anything about hops that aren't its immediate neighbors.

**The receiver (Dave):** Knows that the payment is for him (the route ends with him), and he learns whatever payment metadata Alice included, like an invoice reference. He does *not* know who paid him, unless Alice voluntarily reveals that or includes hints in the invoice.

These properties are deceptively strict. Notice in particular that Bob shouldn't even be able to tell whether *he* is the first hop, the last forwarder, or somewhere in between. This will turn out to drive a lot of the construction we're going to build, because a packet that shrinks at each hop would obviously give away how many hops are left, and a packet whose contents change shape would obviously give away which hop is reading it.

## The threat model

When we build a system for privacy, we need to be clear about who we're defending against. Lightning's onion routing assumes:

**A passive network observer.** Someone who can see traffic between nodes, but can't decrypt it (Lightning's transport encryption from the Noise tutorial handles confidentiality and authentication on each hop's connection). They watch packets fly by and try to figure out who's paying whom.

**A curious forwarder.** A node that follows the protocol (it forwards correctly, doesn't drop packets) but reads everything it can read. Most real forwarders are at least somewhat curious because logging is normal.

**Colluding forwarders.** Multiple nodes along the route comparing notes. Two hops who share what they each saw. If only one forwarder learns anything useful, the colluding case usually still loses. But if every forwarder learns "the payment came from the previous hop," then the chain of "previous hop" links can be assembled into a full path. Onion routing has to break that chain.

A few things are explicitly **not** in scope:

- **Active malicious forwarders.** A node that drops packets, returns wrong errors, or otherwise sabotages the payment. Lightning's HTLC protocol handles this with timelocks and on-chain settlement, not the onion. We'll touch on this when we get to error handling.
- **Timing analysis.** If only one person on the network ever sends payments at 3 a.m., correlating "packet entered at 3:00:01" with "payment confirmed at 3:00:04" can deanonymize them regardless of how the packet is encrypted. Onion routing is about payload privacy, not network-level metadata.
- **Payment hash correlation.** When Dave eventually claims the payment by revealing the **preimage**, every forwarder will see the same payment hash on its incoming and outgoing HTLC. That alone doesn't link Alice to Dave (intermediate hops still don't know either endpoint), but it does create a kind of fingerprint that survives. We'll come back to this in chapter 9.

## What onion routing actually does

Here's the high-level idea, just enough to give you a target.

Alice doesn't write a single plaintext header. Instead, she writes a small piece of routing instruction *for each hop*, encrypts each piece with a key only that hop can derive, and then nests them. Bob's instructions are wrapped in encryption Bob can open. Inside Bob's layer, there's another layer that only Carol can open. Inside Carol's layer, there's one for Dave. The whole thing is exactly the same size at every hop. When Bob peels off his layer, what he forwards to Carol is a packet that looks exactly like the one he received: same length, same shape, no obvious sign of how many layers remain.

That's the metaphor: an **onion**, with each hop peeling one layer, finding instructions for itself, and sending the rest along.

The clever part isn't the metaphor though. It's how Alice constructs all those nested layers when each hop's encryption key is something only that hop can derive. She doesn't have Bob's, Carol's, or Dave's private keys. So how can she build something Bob will be able to decrypt? That's where the cryptography we'll build over the next few chapters comes in.

## What you'll build

By the end of this course, you'll have written:

- A **packet builder** that takes a route plus a payment and produces a real BOLT 4 onion: 1366 bytes, spec-compliant, ready to send.
- A **forwarder** that receives an onion, peels its layer, validates that nothing was tampered with, and forwards the next packet along.
- An **error path** that handles failure gracefully when one of the hops can't process the payment, returning an encrypted error onion that only the sender can decode.
- A working capstone where your code talks to a real test server, sends an actual onion through a simulated three-hop network, and watches each hop unwrap your work in real time.

Before any of that, we need to understand the route itself: how Alice chooses one, how fees and timelocks are calculated, and what she packs into each hop's instructions. That's chapter 2.
