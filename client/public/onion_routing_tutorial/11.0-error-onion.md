# The Error Onion

So far we've spent a lot of time reviewing what happens when payments succeed. This is good, as nowadays (dare I say) most payments do succeed! However, sometimes they do not, so the protocol needs a way to send error messages back, allowing parties to quickly unwind the HTLCs. Additionally, error messages help provide the sender with more information as to why the payment failed. For example, a node might be offline, a channel might lack liquidity, a <g>CLTV</g> might be too tight, or an invoice might have expired. In any case, when something goes wrong, the failing hop should tell Alice why. And it should be able to tell Alice *privately*. 

In this chapter, we'll cover BOLT 4 and learn how a node can route an **<g>error onion</g>** backward through the route, privately informing a sender as to why a payment failed.

## How the error onion works

Let's begin with a high-level review. When a hop decides to fail a payment, it authenticates the **failure message** with its `um` key, encrypts it with its `ammag` keystream, and then hands it to its *upstream* peer. As each upstream hop receives the message, it will add one more `ammag` layer of encryption and pass it along. Each fresh layer makes the error look completely different on every channel it crosses, so it can't be traced as it travels back. If this sounds confusing, don't worry, we'll dig into it in more detail shortly!

Alice doesn't know which hop the error came from, so she finds out by trial and error: peel a layer with Bob's `ammag`, check the result against Bob's `um` HMAC. If there is no match, then she tries again with Charlie's key, etc. Whichever hop's keys produce a valid HMAC, that's the failing hop, and the decrypted bytes are its failure message.

This protocol design provides two important properties:

- **Alice can trust the error.** She can tell exactly which hop generated it, and that it wasn't forged or modified along the way back. Why? Because only the failing hop knows its own `um` key.
- **Nobody else can read it.** The hops between the failing one and Alice can't read what the error says, or even tell where it originated. Each hop only knows its own `ammag` and `um` keys, not anyone else's.

<details>
<summary>Why re-encrypt something that's already encrypted?</summary>

It's not about hiding the contents; the failing hop's layer already does that. It's about making the error look different on every channel it crosses. Passed along unchanged, the same bytes would cross every channel on the way back, and an observer who saw two different channels could match them and stitch the route together. Re-wrapping at each hop means no two channels ever carry correlating bytes, the same property the forward onion gets from changing at every hop.

</details>

## What the failing hop builds

Let's trace what Charlie does when he fails the payment.

#### Step 1. Construct the failure message
First, Charlie constructs the **failure message**: the failure code plus any associated data (like a `channel_update` for `UPDATE`-flagged failures).

#### Step 2. Package it to a fixed size
Next, he packages the message so the error's size can't leak which failure it is. Concretely, he writes four pieces, one after another (260 bytes total in our example):

- `failure_len` (2 bytes): how long the real message is.
- the `failure_message` itself (`failure_len` bytes).
- `pad_len` (2 bytes): how many padding bytes follow.
- the padding itself (`pad_len` bytes). BOLT 4 only constrains the *length* of the pad, not its contents, but senders conventionally zero-fill it (and the spec's own test vector uses `0x00` bytes), so that's what we'll do here.

How much padding? Per BOLT 4, `failure_len + pad_len` must come to at least 256. That way, the message plus its padding stays a constant size no matter how short the real message is, and the two length fields bring the total to 260. Now, what about failure messages *bigger* than 256 bytes, like one carrying a long `channel_update`? For those, `pad_len` simply shrinks toward zero while keeping `failure_len + pad_len` at least 256, so the packet grows with the message. There is no larger fixed size.

#### Step 3. Authenticate with `um`
Then, Charlie computes an HMAC over the 260-byte payload with his `um` key: `error_hmac = HMAC-SHA256(um_charlie, payload)`.

#### Step 4. Prepend the HMAC
After that, he prepends the HMAC to the payload: `error_hmac (32 bytes) || payload (260 bytes)`. That's the full error packet, 292 bytes in all for our example.

<python-snippet id="error-hmac"></python-snippet>

#### Step 5. Wrap with `ammag`
Now, he encrypts the whole packet by XOR-ing it with his `ammag` keystream: `wrapped = error_packet ⊕ ChaCha20(ammag_charlie, len(error_packet))`.

<python-snippet id="error-wrap"></python-snippet>

#### Step 6. Send it upstream
Finally, he sends `wrapped` back to Bob, his upstream hop, as the failure data on the <g>HTLC</g>.

## What the upstream hops do

Now that we've seen what Charlie does, let's review the error onion from Bob's perspective. When Bob receives the error onion, called `wrapped` in our example, from Charlie, he doesn't know what's inside (he doesn't have Charlie's `ammag` key), and BOLT 4 says he doesn't need to. His only job is to wrap one more layer: `wrapped = wrapped ⊕ ChaCha20(ammag_bob, len(wrapped))`. Therefore, he covers however many bytes arrived, then sends the new `wrapped` upstream to Alice.

<python-snippet id="error-rewrap"></python-snippet>

If there were more hops between Alice and Charlie (say, a 5-hop route), each one would wrap with its own `ammag` in turn. By the time the error reaches Alice, it's been XORed with every intermediate hop's keystream plus the originating Charlie's keystream.

## What Alice does

When Alice receives the wrapped error, she has shared secrets <m>ss_AB</m>, <m>ss_AC</m>, <m>ss_AD</m> (one per hop) and can derive the corresponding `um` and `ammag` keys for each. Armed with this cryptographic material, she loops through each hop, starting with the most recent - in this case, Bob. You'll see why she starts with Bob below.

For each hop `i` in order (`i = 0, 1, 2, ..., N-1`), Alice does two things:

1. XOR the wrapped bytes with that hop's keystream, `ChaCha20(ammag_i, len(wrapped))`.
2. Check whether the leading 32 bytes are a valid HMAC over the rest under that hop's `um` key, i.e. whether `HMAC-SHA256(um_i, wrapped[32:])` equals `wrapped[:32]`. If it does, then hop `i` is the one that failed! From here, Alice will read the `failure_len` prefix at the front of the payload and pull out exactly that many bytes as the failure message.

<python-snippet id="error-trial-peel"></python-snippet>

Why does this work? The wrapping order from the network's perspective is: Charlie XORs first, then Bob XORs, then... back to Alice. From Alice's perspective on the return, the *outermost* layer is the most recent hop's wrap (Bob's, in our 3-hop example), and the *innermost* is Charlie's (the failing hop). Alice peels in the order Bob → Charlie, which is `i = 0, 1, ...` since hop 0 is the first forwarder. After XOR-ing with hop <m>i</m>'s `ammag`, if hop <m>i</m> was the failing hop, the contents reveal a 32-byte HMAC that verifies under <m>um_i</m>. If not, Alice will continue.

## One more walkthrough

Now that we've reviewed everything from each of the participants' perspectives, let's review the whole error route, starting with Charlie failing:

1. First, Charlie builds: `error = HMAC(um_charlie, padded_msg) || padded_msg`. Then he wraps: `wire = error ⊕ ammag_charlie_stream` and sends `wire` back to Bob.
2. Next, Bob wraps again: `wire = wire ⊕ ammag_bob_stream` and sends to Alice.

Once Alice receives the error message she will iterate over each layer and attempt to identify the sender and decrypt the error:

3. **Iteration i=0 (Bob's keys):** `peeled = wire ⊕ ammag_bob_stream`. Check `HMAC(um_bob, peeled[32:]) == peeled[:32]`. In our example, Bob isn't the failing hop, so his `um` won't authenticate Charlie's error. So, Alice will continue.
4. **Iteration i=1 (Charlie's keys):** `peeled = peeled ⊕ ammag_charlie_stream`. Now `peeled` is exactly Charlie's original `error` packet, so the HMAC check will validate; `HMAC(um_charlie, peeled[32:]) == peeled[:32]`. At this point, Alice will be able to parse the length prefix at `peeled[32:34]`, find the message length, and read that many bytes starting at `peeled[34:]`.

So, just to hammer things home, the failing hop is identified by **whose `um` key verifies**, which Alice checks by trial-and-decrypt as she peels.
