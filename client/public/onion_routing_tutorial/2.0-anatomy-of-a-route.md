# Anatomy of a Route

Last chapter we said Alice "constructs a route." That phrase glosses over a lot. A route isn't a vague directive like "go through Bob and Carol." It's a precise object Alice builds in her head (or her node's software, anyway) before she touches a single byte of cryptography. This chapter is about exactly what's in it.

We'll keep our same cast: Alice → Bob → Carol → Dave. By the end you'll know what numbers Alice has to compute, how she computes them, and what each hop receives in its slice of the onion.

## A route is a list

At its simplest, a route is an ordered list of hops. For each hop, Alice needs three pieces of public information:

- **The channel to forward on.** Every Lightning channel has a unique `short_channel_id`. If Bob has channels with several other nodes, Alice has to tell him *which* of those channels to use to reach Carol.
- **The fee policy.** Forwarders charge to forward. Each hop publishes its fee as a base amount plus a proportional rate (think: "I take 1 sat plus 0.001% of whatever I'm forwarding"). Alice has to know this in order to compute the right amount to send.
- **The CLTV delta.** Forwarding a payment locks the forwarder's funds for a while; **CLTV** ("CheckLockTimeVerify") is the on-chain mechanism that puts a deadline on that lock. Each hop publishes a delta saying "I require my incoming HTLC to expire at least N blocks after the outgoing one." Alice has to know this too.

Plus a couple of things about the destination:

- **The final amount** Dave should receive (in millisatoshis, the smallest unit Lightning works with).
- **The minimum CLTV** Dave wants on his incoming HTLC (he sets this in the invoice, since he's the one waiting for the preimage to arrive).

Where does Alice get all this? From Lightning's gossip protocol: nodes broadcast `channel_announcement` and `channel_update` messages so other nodes can build a graph of who can route to whom. Pathfinding (choosing *which* route through that graph) is its own topic, big enough that we'll touch it in chapter 13. For everything in between, we'll just assume Alice has a route already and focus on what she does with it.

## Fees and timelocks: working backward

Here's where it gets a little tricky. Let's say Dave wants to be paid 10,000 sats. Carol's fee for forwarding through her channel is 1 sat plus 0.01%, and her CLTV delta is 40 blocks. Bob's fee is 1 sat plus 0.001%, with a CLTV delta of 80 blocks. The current block height is 100, and Dave's invoice asks for a final CLTV of 40 blocks (so the HTLC he receives must be valid until at least block 140).

How much does Alice need to send into the route? And what timelocks does she assign?

The answer is: she works **backward** from Dave.

**Step 1: Set Dave's incoming amount and timelock.**

> Dave's incoming amount: 10,000 sats
> Dave's incoming CLTV: 100 + 40 = block 140

**Step 2: Compute what Carol needs to receive.**

Carol is going to forward 10,000 sats to Dave. Her fee on a 10,000-sat forward is 1 sat (base) + 1 sat (0.01% of 10,000), so 2 sats. So Carol needs to receive 10,002 sats.

Carol also needs her own incoming timelock to expire later than her outgoing one, by at least her CLTV delta of 40 blocks.

> Carol's incoming amount: 10,002 sats
> Carol's incoming CLTV: 140 + 40 = block 180

**Step 3: Compute what Bob needs to receive.**

Bob is forwarding 10,002 sats to Carol. His fee is 1 sat + ~0 sats (0.001% of 10,002 rounds down), so 1 sat. He needs 10,003 sats from Alice.

Bob's CLTV delta is 80 blocks, so his incoming has to outlast his outgoing by 80.

> Bob's incoming amount: 10,003 sats
> Bob's incoming CLTV: 180 + 80 = block 260

**Step 4: That's what Alice sends.**

Alice initiates the payment with 10,003 sats and a CLTV of 260 on the HTLC she gives to Bob.

The reason we have to walk backward is that each hop's required input depends on its output, and the output of every hop except Dave is the input of the next. We need to start at the only fixed point (Dave) and chain backward.

<details>
<summary>Why does CLTV grow toward the sender? Wouldn't it be safer to grow it toward the receiver?</summary>

It might feel intuitive to give Dave the longest timelock (since he's furthest away), but that's the wrong direction. Each forwarder commits funds when it accepts an HTLC and recovers them when it gets the preimage from downstream. If Bob's incoming HTLC expired *before* Carol's outgoing one, Bob could lose money: Alice could time out the HTLC against Bob (refunding herself) while Carol still owes Bob, since her HTLC hasn't expired yet.

By making the incoming HTLC last *longer* than the outgoing one, every hop is guaranteed enough time to settle their downstream HTLC and then pull funds from upstream. That's why timelocks count *down* from the sender's HTLC and stay longest at the start of the route.

## Why forwarders bother

It's worth pausing on a question we glossed over: why does Bob agree to forward at all? He's tying up 10,003 sats of his channel capacity, exposing himself to timelock risk for hundreds of blocks, and doing actual cryptographic work. What's in it for him?

The fee. Bob keeps the difference between his incoming amount (10,003 sats) and his outgoing amount (10,002 sats), which works out to exactly the 1-sat fee we computed. Carol keeps her 2 sats. Multiplied across many payments per day, that's the income that justifies running a forwarding node.

The fee structure is doing real economic work:

- **Compensating for tied-up capital.** When Bob accepts the HTLC, his 10,003 sats of channel liquidity is locked until either the preimage propagates (success) or the timelock expires (refund). He can't use those sats to forward someone else's payment in the meantime.
- **Pricing in risk.** The longer the CLTV, the longer Bob is exposed if something goes wrong downstream. That's why hops with longer CLTV deltas often charge higher fees: time-value-of-money applies even at the millisat scale.
- **Creating a marketplace.** Forwarders publish their fees and CLTV deltas via the gossip protocol. Senders' pathfinding algorithms compare options and pick cheaper routes. A forwarder who charges too much loses traffic to its competitors. One who charges too little can't justify the operational cost.
- **Penalizing bad behavior.** A forwarder that goes offline or refuses to forward isn't earning anything during that time. Senders' implementations also blacklist hops that fail too often, so unreliable forwarders get routed around. Reliability is the product, and fees are the price.

This is why "Bob and Carol are real people running real nodes" wasn't just flavor text in chapter 1. They have skin in the game. The economic incentive to forward correctly is exactly what makes the rest of Lightning's trust model work: you don't need to trust Bob's intentions, just his self-interest.

## What Alice tells each hop

Now that Alice has the numbers, she needs to put them somewhere each hop can read them. The "somewhere" is the **hop payload**: a tiny piece of structured data, intended for one specific hop, that lives inside that hop's layer of the onion.

For an intermediate hop (Bob, Carol), the payload contains:

- **`amt_to_forward`**: how much to forward to the next hop.
- **`outgoing_cltv_value`**: the CLTV value to use on the outgoing HTLC.
- **`short_channel_id`**: which channel to forward on.

For the final hop (Dave), the payload contains:

- **`amt_to_forward`**: how much was sent to him (same as `outgoing_cltv_value`'s relationship with the next hop, this is just his amount).
- **`outgoing_cltv_value`**: the final CLTV (no outgoing HTLC, but still part of the spec for consistency).
- **`payment_secret`** and **`total_msat`**: identifiers from the invoice that prove this payment is the one he requested.

Notice that intermediate hops have a `short_channel_id` and the final hop doesn't. That's the load-bearing difference: a hop reading its payload knows it's the final hop *if and only if* the `short_channel_id` field is missing. Alice doesn't have to tag hops with "you're the last one." The structure of the payload tells them.

## TLV: how the bytes are laid out

In modern Lightning, hop payloads are encoded as **TLV**, which stands for Type-Length-Value. The format is exactly what it sounds like:

```
[ type ][ length ][ value ]
```

Each field gets a numeric type, a length prefix, and the raw bytes of its value. A whole TLV record is just a concatenation of these triples, in increasing type order.

The types Lightning uses for hop payloads are:

| Type | Field | Used by |
|------|-------|---------|
| 2 | `amt_to_forward` | every hop |
| 4 | `outgoing_cltv_value` | every hop |
| 6 | `short_channel_id` | intermediate hops only |
| 8 | `payment_data` (secret + total) | final hop only |

`type` and `length` use **bigsize** encoding, Lightning's variable-length integer format. For values under 0xfd, they're a single byte; for larger ones, they're prefixed with 0xfd, 0xfe, or 0xff and followed by 2, 4, or 8 bytes respectively. Most hop payloads use single-byte type and length fields.

When Dave parses his payload and doesn't see a type 6, he knows: *I'm the destination.* When Bob parses his and sees type 6 with a `short_channel_id`, he knows: *I'm a forwarder; here's where to forward.*

## What each hop ends up with

Putting it all together, when the onion arrives at each hop and that hop peels its layer, here's what they extract:

**Bob extracts:**
> amt_to_forward = 10,002 sats
> outgoing_cltv_value = block 180
> short_channel_id = the channel to Carol

He uses the channel ID to find his channel with Carol, applies whatever local validation he wants (does the CLTV give him enough time? is Carol online? does the channel have enough liquidity?), and forwards an HTLC of 10,002 sats with a CLTV of 180.

**Carol extracts:**
> amt_to_forward = 10,000 sats
> outgoing_cltv_value = block 140
> short_channel_id = the channel to Dave

Same drill, one hop closer.

**Dave extracts:**
> amt_to_forward = 10,000 sats
> outgoing_cltv_value = block 140
> payment_data = (secret + total)

No `short_channel_id`. So Dave knows the buck stops with him. He compares the `payment_data` to the invoice he issued, accepts the HTLC if everything matches, and reveals the preimage to claim the funds.

That's the entire informational content of the route, distributed to the hops that need each piece. Bob never sees Dave's `payment_data`; Dave never sees the `short_channel_id`s; nobody sees anything that wasn't packed for them.

How does Alice make sure each hop only sees their own payload? That's the question chapter 3 answers.
