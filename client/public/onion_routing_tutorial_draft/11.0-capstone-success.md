# Capstone: Successful Payment

You've built a Sphinx packet builder, a forwarder, and an error path. The capstone wires it all together.

In this chapter, your code constructs a real BOLT 4 onion targeting a 3-hop test network: **Alice → Bob → Carol → Dave**. The packet is submitted to a simulated network that peels each layer in turn, broadcasting events as it goes. You'll watch the packet propagate through the route in real time, with each hop's shared secret derivation, payload extraction, and forward action visible step by step.

If everything works, the network returns a **preimage** (the same kind of preimage you'd see settling a real Lightning payment). That preimage is the proof that your packet was correctly constructed, your filler was right, your HMACs verified, and your TLV payloads parsed.

## What you'll see

When you click **Send Payment**, the panel below runs the following sequence:

1. **Builder phase** (your code): your `OnionPacketBuilder.build` runs against the test route's pubkeys, producing a 1,366-byte packet.
2. **Hop 0** (Bob): the network's Bob simulator receives your packet, derives the shared secret with his node privkey, peels his layer, extracts the TLV payload, and emits events for each step.
3. **Hop 1** (Carol): same routine, with Carol's keys, on the packet Bob forwarded.
4. **Hop 2** (Dave): Dave receives, peels his final layer, finds the type-8 `payment_data` (no `short_channel_id` means destination), and verifies it against a pre-registered invoice.
5. **Settlement**: Dave reveals the preimage. Carol claims her HTLC, then Bob, then Alice. The panel surfaces the preimage and a success animation.

If any hop's HMAC verification fails, the run aborts and you'll see a diagnostic indicating which hop rejected the packet. Most often, a wrong HMAC at hop 0 means your builder's chain has a bug in `derive_shared_secrets` or `wrap_hop`. Wrong HMAC at hop 1 usually means filler is broken.

## Reading the events

Each hop emits four events as it peels:

- **received**: the inbound 1,366 bytes have arrived. The hop reports the first 8 bytes of the ephemeral pubkey to confirm the chain is advancing correctly.
- **shared_secret**: ECDH ran, produced 32 bytes. Compare to your builder's chain output to verify they match.
- **payload**: the TLV decoded successfully. Amount, CLTV, and (for forwarders) `short_channel_id` are reported.
- **forwarded** or **delivered**: the hop produced the next packet (forwarders) or accepted the payment (final hop).

The events are useful for debugging. If your packet fails at hop 1, look at hop 0's events: did the shared secret match? Did the payload decode? Was the next ephemeral pubkey correct? Working backward from the failing hop is usually the fastest way to find the bug.

<details>
<summary>What if my code passes the per-exercise tests but fails the capstone?</summary>

The exercise tests check individual functions in isolation. The capstone runs them in concert with each other and with the network's expectations. A few common gaps:

- **Filler off-by-one.** The filler exercise tests against a reference implementation, so getting it right means your output matches byte-for-byte. If the filler is one byte short or long, the per-hop HMAC at the second forwarder will fail. The capstone surfaces this as "hop 1 HMAC verification failed."
- **Bigsize-prefix confusion.** Hop payloads in the build are bigsize-prefixed (a length followed by the TLV bytes). If you forgot the bigsize, the forwarder's parser will misread the payload size and slot.
- **Missing payment_data.** The destination's TLV must include type 8 (`payment_data`). The network's Dave verifies it against the pre-registered invoice; if it's missing or wrong, Dave fails with `incorrect_or_unknown_payment_details`.

In all cases, the panel's per-hop diagnostics are designed to point you at the right exercise to revisit.

## Why this is the capstone

This is the moment your work meets the real spec. Up until now, every exercise verified your output against a Python reference inside the same Pyodide environment. The capstone runs your packet against an independent simulator that follows BOLT 4 directly. If your code interoperates with that simulator, it interoperates with real Lightning.

Real Lightning nodes do exactly what the simulator does: receive a 1,366-byte packet, peel it, validate, forward. Your builder produces packets that any BOLT-4-conformant node would accept and process. That's the bar.
