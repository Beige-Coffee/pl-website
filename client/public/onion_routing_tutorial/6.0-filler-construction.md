# Filler Construction

This is the trickiest chapter in the course. We're going to explain why a piece of the onion called the **filler** has to exist, what bytes go in it, and how Alice computes those bytes during construction. Get this right and the rest of the build is mechanical.

Here's the question we have to answer: when a forwarder peels its layer and shifts the next hop's data forward, **what fills the gap that opens up at the end?**

## What "peeling a layer" actually does

Let's get concrete about what a forwarder does. Bob receives a 1,366-byte packet. He extracts the version byte and the ephemeral pubkey, derives his shared secret and his five keys (chapters 3 and 4), and verifies the HMAC. So far, so good.

Now he has to read his TLV payload and forward what's left. Here's how:

1. **Decrypt the entire 1,300-byte hop_payloads field** by XOR-ing it with a 1,300-byte keystream generated from his `rho` key. The first bytes of the result are his TLV instructions; everything after is still encrypted (it's the next hop's view).
2. **Read his TLV payload** off the front. Suppose his payload + the 32-byte HMAC pointing to the next layer takes up 65 bytes total.
3. **Shift the remaining 1,235 bytes forward** so they start at byte 0 of a fresh 1,300-byte buffer.
4. **Pad the trailing 65 bytes** to bring the buffer back up to its full size.

That last step is the question. Bob has to fill 65 bytes at the end. With *what*?

If he padded with literal zeros, here's the chain of consequences. The bytes he sends to Carol have zeros in their last 65 positions. Carol receives the packet, generates her own 1,300-byte `rho` keystream, and XORs it over the entire field to peel her layer. The last 65 bytes of Carol's XOR result are zero-XORed-with-her-keystream, which is just her keystream. That's a deterministic value, but it isn't what Alice computed Carol's HMAC over. Carol's HMAC verification fails. Payment fails.

So zeros don't work. The trailing bytes of every hop's view of the packet have to be exactly the bytes Alice baked the HMAC over. Those bytes are the **filler**, and Alice has to precompute them before the packet ever leaves her node.

## What the filler has to be

Let's figure out the filler from Alice's perspective.

Imagine Alice has finished construction and is about to send the packet to Bob. She knows what every hop's view of the packet should look like, because she's the one who built each layer. In particular, she knows exactly what the **last `bob_size` bytes of Bob's hop_payloads field** should look like. Why? Because those bytes are what Bob will *shift away* when peeling, and what will end up at the **trailing portion of Carol's hop_payloads field** after Bob's shift.

For Carol's HMAC to verify, those trailing bytes need to look (after Carol's `rho` XOR) like genuine encrypted-then-decrypted bytes. If Bob shifted them forward correctly, they were already once XORed with Bob's `rho` keystream, so they'd need to be XORed with Bob's `rho` again to look "fresh." But they're not. They're going through Carol's XOR, not Bob's.

The trick: Alice precomputes what those trailing bytes *would* look like if every prior hop had XORed them with their keystream as part of peeling. She bakes this into the original packet. It looks like noise to anyone glancing at it, but it has the property that when each hop in turn applies its `rho` keystream over those positions, the cumulative effect is to leave exactly the bytes that Carol's HMAC was computed over.

This isn't intuitive. Let's walk it through.

## The filler algorithm

Here's how Alice computes filler for our route Bob → Carol → Dave. She'll need filler to handle Bob's shift (which affects Carol's HMAC) and Carol's shift (which affects Dave's HMAC). Dave doesn't shift anything, so no filler is generated for the final hop.

**Step 1: Start with empty filler.**

```
filler = b""
```

**Step 2: For Bob (hop 0):**

Append `bob_size` zeros to the filler. This represents the new bytes that Bob's shift will introduce at the end of Carol's view.

```
filler = b"\x00" * bob_size
```

Then generate Bob's `rho` keystream extended to cover both the original 1,300-byte hop_payloads area *and* `bob_size` extra bytes (1,300 + bob_size total). Take the LAST `len(filler)` bytes of that keystream, and XOR them into the filler.

```
stream = chacha20(bob_rho, 1300 + bob_size)
filler = xor(filler, stream[-len(filler):])
```

Now the filler contains the keystream pattern that would be visible at Carol's trailing positions if Bob's shift were treated as a virtual extension of the keystream.

**Step 3: For Carol (hop 1):**

Append `carol_size` more zeros to the filler. The filler now has length `bob_size + carol_size`, representing trailing positions that span back two hops.

```
filler = b"\x00" * carol_size + filler
```

Generate Carol's `rho` keystream extended to cover 1,300 + (bob_size + carol_size) bytes. XOR the last `len(filler)` bytes into the filler.

```
stream = chacha20(carol_rho, 1300 + bob_size + carol_size)
filler = xor(filler, stream[-len(filler):])
```

Now the filler accumulates contributions from both Bob's rho-XOR and Carol's rho-XOR, so when each hop peels in turn, the trailing positions arrive at exactly the bytes Alice baked her HMAC over.

**Step 4: That's it.**

Dave is the final hop. He doesn't forward, so his HMAC is computed over the filler-free portion of the packet, and we don't need to generate any more filler bytes.

## A different way to see it

If the algorithm above made your head hurt, here's an equivalent framing.

Imagine running peeling **in reverse**, from Dave back toward Bob. Dave's view of the trailing portion is "all zeros" (or whatever Alice padded with at the very end of construction). Carol's shift would have introduced those zeros into Dave's trailing bytes. So if we want to know what Carol's trailing bytes should look like, we walk backward from Dave: Carol's shift introduced zeros, so just before Carol's shift, those positions had Carol's `rho` keystream XORed with zeros, which is the keystream itself.

Working backward like this, each hop's trailing-byte pattern is the previous hop's keystream stacked on top of zeros. The forward-direction algorithm above produces the exact same bytes; it just builds them up incrementally instead of peeling them backward.

If you can mentally hold the picture of "each hop's `rho` keystream, layered on top of the next hop's keystream, in the right positions," you've got the filler.

## What this enables

Once Alice has the filler, she'll use it as the seed when she begins constructing the packet (chapter 7). She'll write Dave's TLV payload at the front, place the filler at the end, and then encrypt that. Then she'll prepend Carol's payload (shifting Dave's contents forward in the new buffer), encrypt with Carol's `rho`, and so on. The filler we computed here ensures each layer of encryption produces a packet whose trailing bytes are precisely what the next hop will produce after peeling.

Without the filler, the construction collapses. With it, the construction is a clean recursion.

<details>
<summary>What if the filler bytes are wrong?</summary>

If a hop's trailing bytes don't match what its rho keystream expects, the next hop's HMAC verification will fail. The forwarder will reject the packet (per BOLT 4, with an `invalid_onion_hmac` error). The error onion comes back to Alice, who has the failure and the route to debug.

Practically, this never happens in correct implementations because filler is fully deterministic given the route and the rho keys. But it's the kind of bug that's easy to introduce when implementing Sphinx for the first time: off-by-one errors on the keystream slicing, or padding the wrong direction, will produce a packet that looks well-formed but fails at the very first hop. The test suite for this chapter's exercise checks against a reference implementation to catch these mistakes.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Why doesn't the final hop need filler?</summary>

The final hop doesn't shift anything forward. After it decrypts, it reads its TLV payload, sees that there's no `short_channel_id` (so it's the destination), and stops. There's no "next hop" whose HMAC needs to match anything. Alice still pads the part of the packet beyond Dave's payload during construction (typically with zeros, since there's no further structure), but those zeros never need to align with another hop's rho keystream.

</details>
